# Persuasion Framework for Proposal Evaluation

This file explains how to judge whether a proposal actually persuades.
Not whether it merely contains content.
Not whether it sounds professional.
Whether it builds a credible case that a procurement evaluator can believe, defend, and score well.

Use this file when scoring:
- **Technical Credibility**
- **Competitive Differentiation**
- **Client-Centricity**
- second-order effects on **Executive Readability**, **Honesty & Transparency**, and **Document Flow & Structure**

Cross-reference:
- `setup/reading-psychology.md`
- `setup/structure-patterns.md`
- `setup/writing-standards.md`
- `setup/evaluator-personas.md`

## 1. Proposals are arguments, not information warehouses

A weak proposal treats the document like a storage container:
- answer requirement
- list feature
- insert diagram
- add case study
- end with “why us”

A strong proposal treats the document like an argument:
- establish the client's problem in their terms
- show the proposed solution matches the decision criteria
- prove the claims with credible evidence
- address risks, gaps, and objections before they grow
- leave the evaluator with a defensible reason to shortlist

### The central review question
Do not ask only:
- “Is the information present?”

Also ask:
- “Does the information add up to a persuasive case?”

A proposal can contain the right ingredients and still fail because:
- the claims are not linked to evidence
- evidence is irrelevant or mismatched
- the reasoning between claim and proof is implicit rather than clear
- the client problem is acknowledged but not structurally centered
- differentiators are asserted, not demonstrated

### Reviewer mindset
Your job is not to reward volume.
Your job is to test argumentative strength.

If the proposal reads like a pile of competent fragments instead of a controlled case for selection, that should be visible in the score.

## 2. Aristotle's rhetoric applied to proposals

Aristotle's three persuasive appeals remain useful:
- **Ethos** — why should I trust you?
- **Logos** — why does your reasoning hold?
- **Pathos** — why does this matter to me and my stakes?

Good proposals use all three.
Weak proposals overuse one and neglect the others.

---

## 3. Ethos: the architecture of trust

Ethos in proposals is not personality charm.
It is institutional credibility.
The evaluator asks:
- do these people seem competent?
- do they understand the problem?
- are they honest about limits?
- do they sound like adults, or like marketers?

### Sources of ethos in a proposal
- precise terminology used correctly
- calm, controlled tone
- honest distinction between current capability and roadmap
- relevant evidence and references
- acknowledgement of trade-offs
- clear handling of risk, governance, and implementation realities
- alignment between what is promised and what is actually shown

### False ethos
Some proposals try to simulate ethos with:
- inflated adjectives
- “leading / world-class / best-in-class” claims
- large but contextless client logos
- vague references to “years of experience”
- jargon clouds designed to imply competence

This is not ethos.
It is costume jewelry.

### Review prompts for ethos
- Does the proposal sound credible before I check details?
- Does the evidence support the self-presentation?
- Are limitations stated cleanly, or buried?
- Does the tone create trust or suspicion?
- Are reference points relevant to this client's world?

### Ethos failure patterns
- roadmap described in present tense
- case studies from irrelevant sectors
- claims outpacing proof
- excessive self-praise with weak evidence
- abrupt style changes that suggest patchwork authorship

### Example

**Weak ethos**
“We are the market-leading innovator in digital assets with unmatched expertise across industries.”

**Strong ethos**
“For institutions that require deterministic settlement, role-segregated approval workflows, and multi-jurisdiction control, credibility comes from implementation detail rather than adjectives. This response therefore distinguishes current product capability from roadmap items and references comparable regulated deployments where relevant.”

## 4. Logos: does the case actually hold?

Logos is the logical structure of the proposal's claims.
It answers:
- does the reasoning make sense?
- are the claims supported?
- does the evidence fit the claim?
- are the implied conclusions justified?

Many proposals fail here because they state conclusions without building the bridge to them.

### Common logos failures
- “We are scalable” with no benchmark
- “We understand your environment” with no evidence of tailoring
- “We reduce implementation risk” with no explanation of how
- “We are different” with no comparative proof

### What strong logos looks like
A strong proposal makes the reasoning explicit:
1. client need is defined
2. solution response is stated
3. mechanism is described
4. evidence is supplied
5. implications for the client are made clear

### Example

**Weak**
“Our platform reduces operational risk.”

**Better**
“Your current process relies on manual reconciliation between issuance records, settlement status, and participant approvals. DALP reduces that operational risk by centralising those controls in a single governed workflow: issuance rules are enforced at asset level, settlement events are tracked in real time, and approval actions are logged with role-based permissions. This reduces the number of handoffs where unauthorised or inconsistent actions can occur.”

The second version gives a mechanism and implication.
That is logos.

## 5. Pathos: procurement-safe emotional force

Pathos in proposals is often misunderstood.
It does not mean melodrama.
It means connecting the proposal to the evaluator's real stakes:
- risk of failure
- scrutiny from internal committees
- operational burden
- political visibility
- regulatory exposure
- career risk if the chosen vendor fails

A good proposal does not shout emotion.
It demonstrates that the vendor understands why the decision matters.

### Legitimate pathos in proposal writing
- acknowledging the cost of implementation disruption
- framing time-to-value in operational terms
- showing awareness of regulatory consequences
- understanding internal stakeholder complexity
- explaining what confidence looks like for the client's board, risk, or operations teams

### Illegitimate or weak pathos
- hype language
- fearmongering
- generic “future-proof your business” slogans
- overdramatic transformation claims

### Example

**Weak pathos**
“This transformative solution will revolutionise your digital asset journey.”

**Strong pathos**
“For your operations and risk teams, the issue is not whether a digital asset platform is theoretically capable. It is whether settlement, approvals, and reporting can be governed in a way your institution can defend internally. This response focuses on that operating confidence.”

This is emotional intelligence without theatrics.

## 6. Balancing ethos, logos, and pathos

Proposal teams often over-rotate into one mode.

### Logos without ethos
The document contains detail, but feels dry, patchworked, or evasive.
Result: technically informative, still not trusted.

### Ethos without logos
The document sounds polished and confident, but the proof is thin.
Result: elegant brochure.

### Pathos without logos
The document speaks to the client's stakes, but cannot show how the solution truly addresses them.
Result: empathetic but hollow.

### Strong proposal balance
- ethos: “they are credible”
- logos: “their case makes sense”
- pathos: “they understand why this matters here”

### Review implication
When a proposal feels persuasive or unpersuasive, diagnose which rhetorical engine is missing.
That yields much better feedback than “needs stronger messaging.”

## 7. Toulmin model for proposal analysis

The Toulmin model is one of the most useful tools for evaluating proposal argument quality.
It breaks an argument into:
- **Claim** — what is being asserted
- **Evidence / Data** — what supports it
- **Warrant** — why that evidence supports the claim
- **Backing** — deeper support for the warrant
- **Qualifier** — the limits or conditions
- **Rebuttal** — anticipated objections or exceptions

Use this model aggressively.
Most mediocre proposal writing collapses because it only supplies claims.

---

## 8. Claim: what is the proposal actually saying?

Claims in proposals appear as statements like:
- “The platform supports multi-jurisdiction issuance.”
- “The implementation approach reduces operational risk.”
- “Our architecture is differentiated.”
- “The proposed model is a strong fit for your requirements.”

### Good claim characteristics
- specific
- relevant to the client's criteria
- scoped appropriately
- testable against evidence

### Bad claim characteristics
- vague
- overbroad
- emotionally inflated
- impossible to verify

### Example

**Weak claim**
“We provide a comprehensive platform.”

**Better claim**
“The platform supports issuance, settlement, and approval workflows in one governed operating model, reducing the need for separate orchestration across multiple tools.”

The second claim is clearer and more testable.

---

## 9. Evidence: what proof is supplied?

Evidence may include:
- benchmarks with method
- architecture diagrams
- standards references
- deployment examples
- workflow descriptions
- requirement mapping
- implementation timelines with dependencies
- named controls and operational procedures

### Good evidence
- relevant to the claim
- specific enough to test
- proportionate to the importance of the claim
- close to the claim in the reading path

### Weak evidence
- generic testimonials
- irrelevant case studies
- large numbers without context
- visuals that do not answer the question
- references to “leading institutions” without substance

### Review prompt
Do not just ask whether evidence exists.
Ask whether it is the right evidence for the claim being made.

Example:
- claim: “strong central-bank fit”
- weak evidence: a DeFi startup case study
- strong evidence: governance, control, and comparable regulated deployment detail

---

## 10. Warrant: the missing middle in many proposals

The warrant explains why the evidence supports the claim.
In proposal writing, this is often the missing step.

### Example without warrant
“Settlement occurs in under three seconds. Therefore, operational risk is reduced.”

That leap may be directionally plausible, but the reasoning is incomplete.

### Example with warrant
“Settlement occurs in under three seconds with deterministic finality. Because transactions do not sit in an uncertain pending state awaiting probabilistic confirmation, operations teams spend less time managing settlement ambiguity and exception handling. That is why faster deterministic settlement reduces operational exposure in this process.”

Now the bridge is visible.

### Review implication
When a proposal seems to “jump” from fact to conclusion, check the warrant.
A missing warrant often makes a proposal feel less persuasive even if the raw evidence is decent.

---

## 11. Backing: why the warrant should be believed

Backing strengthens the warrant by showing that the reasoning itself rests on something credible.

Examples of backing:
- standards references
- implementation patterns used in comparable environments
- known operational constraints
- regulatory logic
- architecture rationale

### Example
Claim: “Our approval model reduces governance risk.”
Evidence: “The platform uses role-based approval flows with audit trails.”
Warrant: “Role-based approval reduces risk because critical actions require explicit authorised review.”
Backing: “This matches segregation-of-duties expectations in regulated operational environments and creates an auditable chain for internal and external review.”

Backing matters most when evaluators are skeptical or technically deep.
It turns a sensible claim into a defensible one.

---

## 12. Qualifier: credibility through limits

A qualifier narrows the claim to the circumstances in which it truly holds.
Proposal teams often avoid qualifiers because they fear seeming less strong.
That is a mistake.

### Examples of useful qualifiers
- “currently”
- “in the proposed operating model”
- “for EVM-compatible networks”
- “where the client retains approval control”
- “in production today” vs “targeted for Q3 2026”

### Why qualifiers build trust
They signal control and honesty.
They prevent overclaiming.
They help legal, technical, and procurement readers align on what is actually being promised.

### Weak pattern
“The platform supports all messaging standards.”

### Better pattern
“The platform currently supports X and Y natively. Z requires a translation layer today and is on the roadmap for native support in Q3 2026.”

Qualifier quality should influence:
- **Technical Credibility**
- **Honesty & Transparency**
- **Client-Centricity**

---

## 13. Rebuttal: anticipating evaluator objections

A rebuttal acknowledges likely objections and addresses them before the evaluator raises them.
This is a strong persuasive move when done calmly.

### Common evaluator objections
- “This sounds good, but is it live today?”
- “This may work in theory, but can it operate in our environment?”
- “You claim control, but where is the governance model?”
- “You say this is differentiated, but why does it matter to us?”
- “This case study is interesting, but is it actually comparable?”

### Strong rebuttal pattern
- state the likely concern directly
- answer it with evidence or qualification
- explain why the concern is contained or addressed

### Example
“Native ISO 20022 generation is not currently part of the production release. For institutions that require it immediately, we provide a documented translation-layer approach that two comparable clients use today. Native support is planned for Q3 2026. This means the requirement can be met now, but not yet through a single native module.”

That is a persuasive rebuttal because it answers the objection without pretending it does not exist.

## 14. How to detect whether a proposal argues or merely states

This is one of the most important reviewer distinctions.

A proposal **states** when it:
- lists features
- declares strengths
- repeats adjectives
- answers “yes” with minimal elaboration
- gestures at case studies without linking them to the client's need

A proposal **argues** when it:
- makes a claim tied to the client's problem
- proves the claim with relevant evidence
- explains the logic connecting the two
- qualifies the claim where needed
- anticipates and addresses doubts

### Fast detection test
Look at any paragraph and ask:
- what is the claim?
- what is the proof?
- why does the proof matter?
- what limit or condition is acknowledged?

If those answers are missing, the paragraph is probably asserting, not arguing.

### Bad pattern: assertion stack
“We are scalable, secure, flexible, and client-centric. Our implementation methodology is proven and our solution is highly differentiated.”

This is noise.
It contains almost no usable argumentative structure.

### Better pattern
“Your requirement prioritises operational control over experimental breadth. Our proposal therefore emphasises governed issuance, role-segregated approvals, and deterministic settlement rather than maximal protocol variety. That trade-off is deliberate: in regulated programmes, control discipline matters more than feature sprawl.”

This paragraph argues.
It takes a position.

## 15. Assertion vs demonstration

A proposal can tell the evaluator something.
Or it can show it.
Strong proposals do both.

### Assertion
A direct claim with no or little proof.
Example: “We understand regulated operating environments.”

### Demonstration
A combination of structure, evidence, and explanation that lets the evaluator infer the claim with confidence.
Example: clearly described approval workflows, segregation of duties, auditability, exception handling, and role boundaries that show regulated operating discipline in practice.

### Review heuristic
Whenever the document claims a strength, ask:
- is this asserted?
- demonstrated?
- both?

### Typical weak claims that need demonstration
- scalability
- security
- implementation maturity
- operational resilience
- differentiation
- client understanding

### Example set

**Assertion only**
“We offer robust governance.”

**Partial demonstration**
“We offer role-based permissions and audit logs.”

**Fuller demonstration**
“Governance controls are built into the operating model. Issuance actions require role-based approval, participant onboarding follows a segregated review path, and each material action is recorded in an auditable event trail. For institutions with internal control obligations, that means the governance model is not procedural theater added on top of the platform; it is part of how the platform operates.”

## 16. Narrative arc in proposals

Proposals are not novels, but they do benefit from narrative progression.
A strong narrative arc helps evaluators feel that the document is going somewhere purposeful.

### The basic proposal arc
1. **Setup** — define the client's challenge, context, or decision environment
2. **Tension** — make clear what is difficult, risky, constrained, or important
3. **Resolution** — show how the proposed solution resolves those stakes credibly
4. **Validation** — provide proof, controls, and implementation realism
5. **Closure** — reinforce why this vendor is a credible choice now

Without arc, a proposal feels flat and interchangeable.
With arc, the document accumulates confidence.

### Setup
The opening should establish the evaluator's world, not the vendor's autobiography.

### Tension
A proposal should acknowledge what makes the client's decision hard.
Examples:
- regulatory uncertainty
- integration complexity
- operational control requirements
- timeline pressure
- internal stakeholder coordination

### Resolution
The solution should answer those tensions directly.
Not just describe the platform.
Show how the proposal resolves the specific client challenge.

### Validation
Proof follows resolution.
This is where architecture details, benchmarks, case studies, and qualified commitments matter.

### Closure
The final sections should not dissolve into generic marketing.
They should lock in the case and make the shortlist decision easier.

## 17. Monroe's Motivated Sequence applied to proposals

Monroe's Motivated Sequence is a classic persuasive structure:
1. attention
2. need
3. satisfaction
4. visualisation
5. action

It can be adapted productively for proposal flow.

### 1. Attention
Capture the evaluator with a relevant opening.
Not hype.
Clarity.
Show that the proposal understands the assignment.

### 2. Need
Define the problem and decision stakes.
Explain why the client's requirement matters operationally, commercially, or regulatorily.

### 3. Satisfaction
Present the proposed response.
Explain how the platform and delivery model address the need.

### 4. Visualisation
Help the evaluator picture the future state:
- operating model
- workflow
- implementation phases
- control model
- end-state benefits grounded in their environment

### 5. Action
Make the shortlist decision feel rational and low-regret.
This may appear as:
- a clear next-step frame
- a confident closing summary
- a practical implementation pathway
- a statement of why the vendor is a safe and capable choice

### Review implication
If the document opens weakly, defines need poorly, presents satisfaction generically, or fails to help the evaluator visualise implementation, persuasion suffers even if the content base is acceptable.

## 18. Trust-building patterns vs claim-stacking patterns

This distinction is critical.

### Trust-building pattern
A claim is followed by:
- scope
- evidence
- mechanism
- qualification
- practical implication

Example:
“We currently support asset issuance and transfer controls on EVM-compatible networks. For your use case, that means the approval rules can be embedded at asset level rather than enforced manually outside the platform. Where a client requires a non-EVM network, the business rules can be carried across, but the deployment architecture changes and would need design confirmation during solution validation.”

This builds trust because it is concrete, bounded, and honest.

### Claim-stacking pattern
A paragraph piles up positive claims without evidential structure.

Example:
“Our comprehensive platform is secure, scalable, flexible, future-ready, client-centric, and designed for regulated institutions.”

This erodes trust because:
- nothing is scoped
- nothing is proved
- nothing is qualified
- nothing feels selected for this client

### Review prompts
- Is the paragraph adding confidence or just adding adjectives?
- Are multiple claims being made without any evidence density?
- Does the prose sound like a person who knows, or a team trying to sound impressive?

### Scoring impact
Claim-stacking should reduce:
- **Technical Credibility**
- **Competitive Differentiation**
- **Client-Centricity**

because it signals low argumentative discipline.

## 19. The burden of proof by claim type

Not all claims require equal proof.
The more material the claim, the stronger the evidence burden.

### Low-to-moderate burden claims
- document structure promises
- general implementation approach
- high-level process framing

### Medium burden claims
- support for a named capability
- ability to integrate with client systems
- suitability for regulated environments

### High burden claims
- performance figures
- security or resilience assertions
- major differentiation claims
- deployment maturity
- statements about compliance or legal enforceability

### Review rule
Do not let high-stakes claims pass with low-stakes proof.

Example:
- high-stakes claim: “supports central-bank-grade governance”
- weak proof: generic role-based access mention
- proper proof: governance workflow, approval rules, auditability, operating model, and ideally relevant deployment context

## 20. Competitive differentiation as argument

Differentiation is not a slogan at the end of the document.
It is a comparative argument woven through the proposal.

### Strong differentiation logic
1. identify a client-relevant decision criterion
2. explain the realistic alternatives or trade-offs
3. show how this proposal handles that criterion differently
4. prove why the difference matters here

### Weak differentiation logic
- “industry-leading”
- “end-to-end”
- “best-in-class”
- “comprehensive”

None of those mean anything without comparative context.

### Example
**Weak differentiation**
“Our platform is highly flexible.”

**Stronger differentiation**
“Many platforms require the ledger choice to be locked early because business rules are tightly coupled to network-specific implementation. Our architecture separates asset lifecycle logic from ledger deployment decisions. For your programme, that reduces the risk of having to redesign the operating model if regulatory or infrastructure preferences change during implementation.”

Now the differentiator is comparative, mechanistic, and client-relevant.

## 21. Client-centricity as argument quality

Client-centricity is not just mentioning the client name.
It is argumentative alignment with the client's world.

### A client-centric argument does these things
- mirrors the client's language
- selects proof that matters in the client's sector
- frames benefits in the client's operating reality
- addresses the client's likely objections
- prioritises the client's stated criteria over generic product talking points

### Non-client-centric argument patterns
- using irrelevant case studies
- describing platform capabilities in generic feature terms
- talking about the vendor's goals instead of the client's risks
- answering the question you wish had been asked

### Review prompt
If I replaced the client name and kept the rest, would the argument still read naturally?
If yes, client-centricity is weak.

## 22. Evaluating objection handling

Good proposals do not wait passively for objections.
They pre-handle them.

### Typical objection categories
- capability gap
- implementation risk
- integration burden
- governance/control concern
- compliance ambiguity
- timeline realism
- vendor fit or sector relevance

### Strong objection handling
- visible
- proportionate
- honest
- near the relevant claim
- resolved with specifics, not spin

### Weak objection handling
- hidden in footnotes
- euphemistic
- deferred to “future discussions”
- contradicted elsewhere
- buried after generic reassurance

### Example
**Weak**
“Any remaining requirements can be addressed during implementation.”

**Strong**
“Requirement 6.4 is only partially covered today. The current release supports approval workflows and auditability, but not the client's requested dual-signature override path. That pattern can be configured through workflow extension during implementation, but it is not available out of the box in the current release. This should be treated as a controlled extension, not as native capability.”

This is persuasive because it prevents later disappointment.

## 23. Common logical defects in proposal arguments

### Non sequitur
The conclusion does not follow from the evidence.

Example:
“We have offices in four countries, therefore we are the best partner for your settlement modernisation.”

### Circular reasoning
The claim is justified by a restatement of itself.

Example:
“Our implementation approach is proven because it is a best-practice implementation methodology.”

### False precision
The document uses precise numbers without support, context, or justified certainty.

Example:
“The implementation will take exactly 11.5 weeks.”

### Overgeneralisation
A narrow success is presented as proof of broad capability.

Example:
“One tokenisation pilot becomes proof of broad multi-jurisdiction operating maturity.”

### Category mistake
Evidence from one context is used to prove fit in another, mismatched context.

Example:
“A startup deployment is used as proof for sovereign infrastructure suitability.”

### Unqualified universals
Words like “all,” “fully,” “always,” or “seamless” are used where conditions obviously apply.

### Review implication
Logical defects should be named plainly in feedback.
Do not soften them into generic “needs more support.”

## 24. Good and bad argumentative examples

### Example A: capability claim

**Bad**
“Our platform provides enterprise-grade workflow orchestration.”

**Why weak**
- claim is abstract
- evidence absent
- no client implication
- adjective-driven

**Better**
“For approval-heavy operating models, the platform executes durable workflows across issuance, participant onboarding, and settlement exception handling. That matters in your environment because critical actions do not rely on manual email coordination or ad hoc intervention. Each workflow step is governed, stateful, and auditable.”

### Example B: client understanding

**Bad**
“We understand the needs of regulated institutions.”

**Better**
“Your RFP places unusual emphasis on control handoffs between operations, compliance, and legal. That suggests the winning solution must do more than automate settlement; it must make governance legible to multiple internal stakeholders. This response therefore treats operating control as a first-class requirement rather than a supporting feature.”

### Example C: differentiation

**Bad**
“Our end-to-end platform is unique in the market.”

**Better**
“The differentiator is not that the platform covers more buzzwords. It is that asset lifecycle rules, approval controls, and ledger deployment choices can be handled within one governed architecture. For buyers trying to avoid fragmented delivery accountability, that integrated control model matters more than raw feature count.”

## 25. How this should influence scoring

### Technical Credibility
Score this lower when:
- major claims lack evidence
- evidence is irrelevant to the claim
- qualifiers are absent where they should exist
- objections are not addressed
- architectural reasoning is implicit or hand-wavy

Score this higher when:
- claims are supported with specific proof
- warrants are visible
- trade-offs are acknowledged
- limits are stated clearly
- the evaluator can understand not just what the platform does, but why the proposal's claims should be believed

### Competitive Differentiation
Score this lower when:
- differentiation exists only as adjectives
- comparative logic is missing
- client relevance is not shown
- evidence does not support the claimed advantage

Score this higher when:
- differentiation is woven into the substance
- realistic alternatives or trade-offs are addressed
- the advantage is explained in client terms
- the evaluator could repeat the differentiator in committee without embarrassment

### Client-Centricity
Score this lower when:
- the argument is generic
- benefits are framed from the vendor's perspective
- the proposal fails to engage the client's decision stakes
- evidence and case studies do not fit the client's world

Score this higher when:
- the document mirrors the client's criteria and language
- pathos is procurement-safe and real
- objections likely to arise in the client's environment are pre-handled
- the proposal feels designed for this buyer, not for any buyer

## 26. Reviewer checklist for argument quality

Use this when scoring persuasive strength.

### Toulmin checklist
- [ ] What is the claim?
- [ ] What evidence supports it?
- [ ] Is the warrant visible or merely implied?
- [ ] Is there backing for the warrant where needed?
- [ ] Is the claim properly qualified?
- [ ] Are likely objections anticipated and handled?

### Rhetoric checklist
- [ ] Does the tone build ethos or simulate it?
- [ ] Is the logic legible?
- [ ] Does the proposal connect to the evaluator's real stakes without hype?

### Anti-assertion checklist
- [ ] Are adjectives replacing proof?
- [ ] Are there claim stacks with no evidence density?
- [ ] Does the proposal demonstrate strengths or merely name them?
- [ ] Could the evaluator defend this argument in committee?

---

## 27. How to write feedback on persuasion defects

Prefer:
- “This section states that the platform reduces operational risk but never explains the mechanism, so the claim reads as assertion rather than argument.”
- “The evidence is real but mismatched: the case study does not support the sovereign-infrastructure claim being made.”
- “The paragraph has a claim and a data point, but the warrant is missing. Explain why that benchmark matters to the client's operating model.”
- “The proposal stacks differentiators without comparative logic. Pick the two that matter most for this client and prove them.”
- “This reads as polished confidence rather than demonstrated credibility. Add qualification and supporting detail.”

Avoid:
- “Make it more persuasive.”
- “Strengthen messaging.”
- “Add more proof.”

That is too vague.

---

## 28. Bottom line

Strong proposals do not just answer questions.
They build a case.

That case should show:
- **ethos** — we can trust these people
- **logos** — their reasoning holds
- **pathos** — they understand why this decision matters here

And inside each major claim, the proposal should show the bones of a real argument:
- claim
- evidence
- warrant
- backing
- qualifier
- rebuttal

If those bones are missing, the proposal may still look polished.
It should not score as persuasive.
