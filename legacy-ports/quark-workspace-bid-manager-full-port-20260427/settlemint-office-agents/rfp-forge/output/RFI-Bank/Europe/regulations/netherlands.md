# Netherlands

Last updated: March 2026

**Country:** Netherlands  
**Regulatory Authority:** Authority for the Financial Markets (AFM) and De Nederlandsche Bank (DNB)

## Key Regulations

### Dutch AML registration regime for crypto service providers
- **Date enacted / effective:** Implemented in 2020 under the Anti-Money Laundering and Anti-Terrorist Financing Act
- **Summary:** Before MiCA, the Netherlands supervised many crypto businesses through DNB registration under AML law. That regime emphasized customer due diligence, sanctions compliance, and integrity risk controls rather than a full prudential crypto licensing framework.
- **Scope:** AML supervision of custody, exchange, and related crypto services.

### Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA) - Regulation (EU) 2023/1114
- **Date enacted / effective:** Entered into force 29 June 2023; fully applicable 30 December 2024
- **Summary:** MiCA has become the main licensing framework for Dutch crypto-asset services. AFM's current crypto-companies page states that the AFM is responsible for CASP license applications under Article 63 MiCAR and for notifications from other financial institutions under Article 60 MiCAR.
- **Scope:** CASP licensing, notifications, conduct, white papers, stablecoins, and cross-border activity.

### Dutch integrity and sanctions framework
- **Date enacted / effective:** Ongoing
- **Summary:** Even after MiCA, the Dutch market remains strongly shaped by AML, sanctions, and integrity expectations. That means transaction monitoring, customer risk scoring, governance, outsourcing controls, and recordkeeping remain commercially decisive.
- **Scope:** AML/KYC, sanctions, governance, and operational controls.

## Licensing Requirements
Providers offering crypto-asset services in the Netherlands must now hold an AFM license, a valid notification route under Article 60 MiCA, or authorization from another EU supervisory authority that can be passported into the Dutch market. DNB remains relevant for the Dutch supervisory environment because the market is still deeply influenced by the legacy AML and integrity framework.

## Taxonomy
The Netherlands follows MiCA's distinction between ARTs, EMTs, and other crypto-assets. Transferable securities, investment products, certain e-money products, and other carved-out instruments remain subject to separate financial-services analysis, so classification still drives architecture.

## Key Compliance Requirements
Dutch procurement teams will expect strong evidence on AML/KYC, sanctions screening, travel-rule controls, governance, outsourcing, cybersecurity, safeguarding, and evidential audit trails. The Netherlands is not a market where vague compliance statements survive diligence.

## Recent Developments
- **30 December 2024:** Full MiCA application began, moving the Dutch market beyond the former DNB AML-registration-only model.
- **2025:** AFM published its dedicated MiCAR crypto-companies supervision page and confirmed that it is the authority handling CASP license applications and Article 60 notifications.
- **2025-2026:** The Netherlands has continued to converge around a two-speed reality: MiCA licensing at the EU level, with a distinctly Dutch emphasis on sanctions, integrity, and financial-crime controls.
- **Current market effect:** Dutch firms and buyers now need to assess not only whether a provider is technically functional, but whether it can survive AFM licensing scrutiny and Dutch integrity expectations.

## Impact on RFP/Procurement
In Dutch RFPs, compliance architecture is often the product. Vendors that can demonstrate sanctions workflows, auditability, role segregation, and institutional controls will be favored over stacks that still look like consumer-crypto tooling with enterprise packaging bolted on.
