# Nigeria

Last updated: March 2026

**Country:** Nigeria
**Regulatory Authority:** SEC Nigeria, Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN), NFIU/SCUML

## Key Regulations

### SEC Rules on Issuance, Offering Platforms and Custody of Digital Assets
- **Date enacted / effective:** Initial 2022 framework; amendments and expansion continued into 2024
- **Summary:** Nigeria's SEC has created one of Africa's more explicit digital-asset frameworks, covering issuance platforms, exchanges, custody, and VASPs. The rules are still maturing, but they provide a clearer legal path than the earlier policy vacuum.
- **Scope:** Digital asset issuance, exchanges, custody, VASP operations.

### CBN policy shift on bank dealings with VASPs
- **Date enacted / effective:** Restrictions eased in late 2023/2024 compared with earlier prohibitions
- **Summary:** The CBN historically restricted bank support for crypto businesses, creating major operating friction. Recent easing has improved practical viability, although banking relationships and controls remain highly important.
- **Scope:** Banking access, fiat rails, operational viability for VASPs.

### AML/CFT framework
- **Date enacted / effective:** Ongoing
- **Summary:** Nigeria requires strong AML/CFT controls around virtual-asset activity, including customer due diligence, suspicious transaction monitoring, and sanctions compliance. Given fraud concerns, regulators expect visible control maturity.
- **Scope:** AML/KYC, monitoring, sanctions, reporting.

## Licensing Requirements
Entities offering exchanges, custody, issuance platforms, or other covered digital-asset services may need SEC authorization/registration, while banking access and payment interfaces require careful coordination with CBN expectations.

## Taxonomy
Nigeria uses the language of digital assets and VASPs, with securities-style treatment where tokenized investment products are involved. The regulatory model is increasingly activity-based.

## Key Compliance Requirements
AML/KYC, sanctions, custody controls, local reporting, governance, cybersecurity, and strong fiat-rail controls are essential. Procurement teams should also expect questions about fraud prevention and customer-risk management.

## Recent Developments
2024-2025 has been about reopening bank connectivity and making the SEC framework more operational. Nigeria remains high-potential but fast-moving, so regulatory monitoring should be built into the operating model.

## Impact on RFP/Procurement
Nigerian enterprise buyers need flexible compliance infrastructure and strong banking-interface controls. Vendors that can demonstrate fraud controls, entity segregation, and policy adaptability will be easier to procure than rigid global templates.
