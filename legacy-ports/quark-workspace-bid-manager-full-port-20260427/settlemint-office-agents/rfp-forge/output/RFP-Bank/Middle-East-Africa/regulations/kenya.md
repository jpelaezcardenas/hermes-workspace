# Kenya

Last updated: March 2026

**Country:** Kenya
**Regulatory Authority:** Capital Markets Authority (CMA), Central Bank of Kenya (CBK), Parliament/Ministry of Finance

## Key Regulations

### Proposed Virtual Asset Service Providers Bill
- **Date enacted / effective:** Drafting and policy development active through 2024-2025
- **Summary:** Kenya has been moving toward a dedicated VASP law but has not yet finalized a full licensing framework. The proposed legislation aims to bring exchanges, custodians, and related businesses into a formal supervisory perimeter.
- **Scope:** Prospective licensing of VASPs, exchanges, custody, AML controls.

### Tax measures on digital asset transfers
- **Date enacted / effective:** Recent finance acts introduced tax treatment
- **Summary:** Kenya has already used tax legislation to recognize and capture digital-asset activity even before a complete market-law framework is in place. This affects transaction economics and reporting obligations for businesses operating locally.
- **Scope:** Taxation, reporting, economic treatment of digital-asset transfers.

### Existing AML and payments constraints
- **Date enacted / effective:** Ongoing
- **Summary:** In the absence of a complete crypto act, general AML/CFT expectations and banking/payment risk controls remain the main operational constraints. Enterprises must plan for conservative financial-institution counterparties.
- **Scope:** AML/KYC, banking-interface controls, monitoring, reporting.

## Licensing Requirements
Kenya does not yet have a fully settled omnibus VASP licence, so businesses operate in a developing policy environment. Enterprises should expect forthcoming licensing, AML registration, and tax-reporting obligations to tighten over time.

## Taxonomy
Kenya has not finalized a detailed token taxonomy. Current policy discussions generally treat virtual assets functionally, distinguishing service-provider activity from securities or payments use cases as needed.

## Key Compliance Requirements
AML/KYC, tax reporting, transaction monitoring, governance, and the ability to adapt quickly to legislative change are essential. Procurement should also consider bank-relationship and fiat-rail constraints.

## Recent Developments
2024-2025 has been defined by draft legislation and increasing policy seriousness. Kenya is not yet a finished rulebook market, but directionally it is moving toward formalization rather than permissiveness.

## Impact on RFP/Procurement
Kenyan buyers should prefer configurable platforms that can absorb new licensing and tax rules without major redesign. Procurement risk is lower when the vendor can show a roadmap for rapid localization and policy updates.
