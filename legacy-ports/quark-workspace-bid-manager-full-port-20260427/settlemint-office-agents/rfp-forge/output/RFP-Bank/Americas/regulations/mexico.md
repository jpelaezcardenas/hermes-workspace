# Mexico

Last updated: March 2026

**Country:** Mexico
**Regulatory Authority:** Banco de México, CNBV, Ministry of Finance

## Key Regulations

### Fintech Law (Ley Fintech)
- **Date enacted / effective:** Enacted 2018
- **Summary:** Mexico's Fintech Law created the basic legal opening for regulated fintech institutions to operate with virtual assets, subject to central bank authorization. The approach is deliberately cautious and channels most lawful activity through regulated institutions rather than a broad open-market crypto licence.
- **Scope:** Fintech institutions, certain virtual-asset operations, regulatory perimeter.

### Banco de México virtual asset rules and circulars
- **Date enacted / effective:** 2019 onward
- **Summary:** Banco de México has limited the use of virtual assets by regulated financial institutions to internally authorized operations, emphasizing risk containment. This has constrained retail expansion while allowing controlled experimentation.
- **Scope:** Regulated financial institutions and approved virtual-asset uses.

### AML framework for vulnerable activities
- **Date enacted / effective:** Ongoing
- **Summary:** Mexico's AML regime remains relevant for crypto-adjacent businesses, particularly around customer identification, reporting, and suspicious transaction controls. The market therefore operates with both fintech and financial-crime constraints.
- **Scope:** AML/KYC, reporting, beneficial ownership, suspicious activity.

## Licensing Requirements
There is no broad omnibus retail VASP licence comparable to MiCA or Bermuda. Most lawful institutional activity requires operation through a licensed fintech or financial institution with Banxico/CNBV involvement, plus AML compliance and product-specific approvals.

## Taxonomy
Mexico uses the term “virtual assets” but does not rely on a broad classification hierarchy. In practice, the key distinction is between authorized regulated use within the financial system and unregulated/open crypto activity outside it.

## Key Compliance Requirements
AML/KYC, reporting, internal controls, customer disclosures, restricted product permissions, and close governance over any institutional use of virtual assets are central. Procurement teams should assume conservative approval and risk-committee processes.

## Recent Developments
2024-2025 did not produce a dramatic liberalization; Mexico remains cautious, especially for bank-facing activity. Institutional projects continue, but the compliance burden remains high relative to market openness.

## Impact on RFP/Procurement
For procurement, Mexico rewards vendors that can support tightly permissioned use cases, layered approval workflows, and conservative product gating. Platforms built for broad retail-market flexibility are usually a poor fit for Mexican institutional buyers.
