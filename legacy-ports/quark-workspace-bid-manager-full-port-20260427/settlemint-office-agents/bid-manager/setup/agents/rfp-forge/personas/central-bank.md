# Buyer Persona: Central Bank or Monetary Authority

**Archetype:** Reserve Bank of India (RBI), Bank Negara Malaysia (BNM), Saudi Central Bank (SAMA), Banque de France, Bank of Thailand, Central Bank of UAE (CBUAE), Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS).

## Profile

The national monetary authority responsible for financial stability, monetary policy, and often payment system oversight. Exploring or piloting CBDC (retail and/or wholesale), tokenized government securities, or modernized payment infrastructure. Operating under extreme scrutiny, any technology failure could undermine public trust in the national currency. Procurement involves multiple government stakeholders beyond the central bank itself (finance ministry, national cybersecurity agency, data protection authority).

## Priorities

1. **Financial stability and systemic risk**: the solution must not introduce systemic risk. Failure modes must be analyzed exhaustively. No single vendor dependency that could threaten national payment infrastructure.
2. **Scalability to national population**: retail CBDC means tens of millions of users. Wholesale CBDC means every bank in the country. The platform must scale accordingly with proven benchmarks.
3. **Privacy and data protection**: CBDC raises unique privacy concerns. The solution must support configurable privacy models (full anonymity for small transactions, KYC for large ones, regulatory visibility).
4. **Offline capability**: for retail CBDC: must function in areas with limited connectivity. This is a hard requirement in many developing economies.
5. **Technology sovereignty**: central banks are extremely sensitive to foreign technology dependency. Open-source components, source code access, and local operational capability are strongly preferred.

## Evaluation Culture

Multi-stakeholder governance with political dimensions. Evaluation may involve external advisory panels, international organization consultation (BIS, IMF, World Bank), and peer central bank reference checks. Procurement timelines are long (12–24 months) with extensive due diligence. Pilot phases are standard and may run for 12+ months before production decision. Budget is not the primary constraint, getting it right is.

## Red Lines

- No proprietary lock-in without source code escrow and exit strategy
- No solutions that haven't been stress-tested at national-scale volumes
- No vendors without willingness to submit to central bank audit and oversight
- No architectures that compromise monetary policy transmission
- No platforms without demonstrated offline transaction capability (for retail CBDC)

## Common RFP Patterns

50–80 page RFPs, often developed with BIS Innovation Hub or similar advisory support. Include detailed scenario-based requirements (crisis scenarios, monetary policy transmission, cross-border interoperability). Require vendors to propose a phased pilot-to-production roadmap. Include knowledge transfer and capacity building as scored requirements. May require establishing a dedicated team within the central bank's premises.
