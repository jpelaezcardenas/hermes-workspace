# Lessons Learned

This file captures recurring lessons from past proposal reviews.
Read it before every new review.

## How to use this file
- Add durable lessons, not one-off noise
- Focus on patterns that should influence future scoring or review emphasis
- When five or more reviews reveal a recurring issue, promote it clearly
- Date new entries

## Seed lessons from improvement iterations 1-7

### 2026-03-14 — Early scan quality is a scoring issue, not a cosmetic issue
- **Pattern observed:** Strong content routinely underperforms when headings, first sentences, and visible proof blocks do not expose the case early.
- **Why it matters:** Evaluators form a trust and effort judgment during the skim. Buried strengths do not behave like strengths in real procurement reading.
- **What to check next time:** Run the heading/first-sentence/diagram-title test before rewarding completeness.
- **Scoring impact:** Executive Readability, Document Flow & Structure, Competitive Differentiation.

### 2026-03-14 — Argument presence is not argument quality
- **Pattern observed:** Many proposals contain the right topics but fail to connect claim, evidence, and implication.
- **Why it matters:** A proposal can be technically complete and still unpersuasive if the reasoning bridge is missing.
- **What to check next time:** Use a Toulmin-style check on major claims: claim, grounds, warrant, qualifier, rebuttal.
- **Scoring impact:** Technical Credibility, Honesty & Transparency, Competitive Differentiation.

### 2026-03-14 — Mixed-audience writing beats specialist showing-off
- **Pattern observed:** Technical sections often drift into vocabulary and density that a business sponsor or procurement manager will not process efficiently.
- **Why it matters:** The document is scored by a committee, not a single specialist. Overly compressed expert prose creates unnecessary friction and weak recall.
- **What to check next time:** Apply the Hemingway principle: write one level below the audience's comprehension ceiling.
- **Scoring impact:** Writing Quality, Executive Readability, Client-Centricity.

### 2026-03-14 — Repetition usually signals weak editorial control
- **Pattern observed:** Teams often repeat differentiators, strengths, and client pain points instead of developing them.
- **Why it matters:** Evaluators do not experience this as reinforcement. They experience it as genericity, padding, or stitched drafting.
- **What to check next time:** Distinguish strategic reinforcement from duplicated phrasing. Ask whether each reappearance adds new proof or implication.
- **Scoring impact:** Writing Quality, Document Flow & Structure.

### 2026-03-14 — Paragraph quality predicts trust more than sentence polish
- **Pattern observed:** Many proposal sections contain acceptable sentences but weak paragraph architecture.
- **Why it matters:** Evaluators read in chunks. If topic sentence, evidence, implication, and transition are missing, comprehension and trust both suffer.
- **What to check next time:** Inspect whether paragraphs are designed or merely accumulated.
- **Scoring impact:** Writing Quality, Executive Readability, Technical Credibility.

### 2026-03-14 — Format choice is part of writing quality
- **Pattern observed:** Weak proposals often force everything into prose, or overuse tables and bullets where narrative reasoning is required.
- **Why it matters:** The wrong container creates friction even when the underlying content exists.
- **What to check next time:** Decide whether prose, table, or diagram is the optimal format for the specific information type.
- **Scoring impact:** Writing Quality, Visual Communication, Document Flow & Structure.

### 2026-03-14 — Voice fractures expose multi-author patchwork
- **Pattern observed:** Proposal sections often shift between sales copy, technical notes, legal caution, and operational filler.
- **Why it matters:** Evaluators infer weak editorial control and inconsistent claim verification when the document sounds assembled rather than integrated.
- **What to check next time:** Compare terminology, certainty level, sentence rhythm, and pronoun use across major sections.
- **Scoring impact:** Writing Quality, Honesty & Transparency, Technical Credibility.

### 2026-03-14 — Ambiguity is often the real defect behind “polished” prose
- **Pattern observed:** Broad language frequently hides uncertainty around scope, timeline, capability status, or responsibility.
- **Why it matters:** Ambiguity forces evaluators to guess. In procurement, guesswork becomes distrust.
- **What to check next time:** Run an ambiguity check on major claims: native vs configurable vs integration-dependent vs roadmap; now vs later; vendor vs client responsibility.
- **Scoring impact:** Honesty & Transparency, Requirement Coverage, Technical Credibility.

### 2026-03-14 — Persona asymmetry matters
- **Pattern observed:** Some proposals are persuasive to one evaluator type but fragile under another lens.
- **Why it matters:** Committees do not score as one blended reader. The strongest internal skeptic can materially affect the outcome.
- **What to check next time:** Pressure-test each major section through technical, business, risk/compliance, procurement, and legal personas.
- **Scoring impact:** Client-Centricity, Requirement Coverage, Technical Credibility, Executive Readability.

### 2026-03-14 — Defect severity must be explicit
- **Pattern observed:** Reviews become less useful when all defects are described in the same tone.
- **Why it matters:** Teams need to know what is polish, what is confidence erosion, and what is a submission blocker.
- **What to check next time:** Name the defect, explain evaluator impact, and classify severity where the distinction helps prioritisation.
- **Scoring impact:** All dimensions through better calibration and clearer review actionability.

### 2026-03-15 -- Accurate DALP claims should score as passing, not be hedged
- **Pattern observed:** Reviews sometimes forced partial scores or found negatives in content that accurately reflected DALP capabilities, creating a bias toward finding problems even when none existed.
- **Why it matters:** Forcing negatives on accurate content undermines review credibility and creates unnecessary revision cycles. If the content is factually correct and properly qualified, it should score well.
- **What to check next time:** Before flagging a DALP capability claim as weak, verify it against the capability-mapping documentation. If the claim is accurate, score it as passing. Do not invent concerns about content that correctly represents the product.
- **Scoring impact:** Technical Credibility, Honesty and Transparency.

### 2026-03-15 -- Review feedback must be specific and actionable
- **Pattern observed:** Reviews sometimes contained vague feedback like "could be better," "needs more detail," or "tighten this up." This gives the revision team nothing to work with.
- **Why it matters:** The purpose of a review is to produce a better next draft. Vague feedback fails that purpose.
- **What to check next time:** Every criticism must include: (1) what specifically is wrong, (2) why it matters for evaluator impact, (3) what specific change would fix it. Replace "needs more detail" with "Section 4 describes the architecture in generic terms but never shows integration points to the client's core systems. Add a solution diagram with labelled interfaces and message directions."
- **Scoring impact:** All dimensions through better review actionability.

### 2026-03-15 -- DOCX formatting is not subject to review
- **Pattern observed:** Reviews occasionally commented on Word document formatting, template styling, or visual presentation aspects controlled by the bid-manager template.
- **Why it matters:** The DOCX template and styling are locked. Commenting on them creates noise and confusion about what the proposal team can actually change.
- **What to check next time:** Focus exclusively on content quality. If a formatting issue is actually a content issue (e.g., wrong heading hierarchy affecting argument flow), frame it as a content/structure issue, not a formatting issue.
- **Scoring impact:** None directly -- this is a process discipline issue.

### 2026-03-15 -- Verify leadership titles in every review
- **Pattern observed:** Proposals sometimes use incorrect titles for leadership team members.
- **Why it matters:** Incorrect titles in a client-facing document are embarrassing and signal sloppy quality control.
- **What to check next time:** Always verify: Matthew Van Niekerk = Co-founder and President, Adam Popat = CEO. Flag any deviation as a Major defect.
- **Scoring impact:** Honesty and Transparency, Client-Centricity.

### 2026-03-15 -- Content guardrails are mandatory
- **Pattern observed:** Proposals have disclosed team size, internal process details, or other confidential information.
- **Why it matters:** Disclosing team size or AI workflow details can undermine client confidence and reveal competitive vulnerabilities.
- **What to check next time:** Check every proposal against the content guardrails in dalp-claim-verification.md. Team size, AI workflow, internal tooling, revenue, and organizational structure are never appropriate in client-facing documents.
- **Scoring impact:** IP and Confidentiality, Honesty and Transparency.

## Feedback Language Standards

When writing review comments, follow these rules:

### Always include
1. **Location**: Where in the document the issue appears (section, page, paragraph)
2. **Problem**: What specifically is wrong (name the defect type)
3. **Impact**: Why this matters for evaluator trust or scoring
4. **Fix**: What specific change would resolve the issue

### Never use
- "Could be better" (say what specifically needs to change)
- "Needs more detail" (say what detail is missing and why it matters)
- "Consider revising" (say what the revision should look like)
- "Tighten this up" (say what is loose and how to fix it)
- "Strengthen the messaging" (say what the message should be)
- "Add more proof" (say what proof is needed and where it should go)

### Preferred patterns
- "Section X claims [capability] but provides no mechanism. Add [specific evidence type] to support this claim."
- "The executive summary opens with vendor biography rather than client challenge. Rewrite the first paragraph to reference [client's stated programme/requirement]."
- "This paragraph uses present tense for a Q3 2026 roadmap item. Change to: 'Targeted for Q3 2026, this capability will...' "
- "The compliance matrix answers Requirement 4.3 with bare 'Yes.' Add the mechanism, evidence reference, and any conditions."
