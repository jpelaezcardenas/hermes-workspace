# Executive Summary Assessment Criteria

This file provides specific criteria for evaluating executive summaries beyond the general scoring rubric.

---

## What an Executive Summary Must Do

An executive summary has four jobs:
1. **Frame the client's challenge** in their own terms
2. **State the proposed response** clearly and specifically
3. **Provide early proof signals** that build confidence
4. **Create momentum** for the evaluator to read further

If the executive summary does not accomplish all four, it is underperforming regardless of how well it is written.

---

## Structural Requirements

### Opening (first 2-3 sentences)
Must reference the client's specific challenge, programme, or stated objective.
Must NOT open with:
- Company founding date, history, or office locations
- Generic industry commentary ("In today's evolving landscape...")
- Product feature lists
- Self-congratulatory statements

### Body (core paragraphs)
Must include:
- Clear articulation of the proposed solution approach
- At least one specific, verifiable proof point (deployment reference, benchmark, or control mechanism)
- Connection between DALP capabilities and the client's stated evaluation criteria
- Honest acknowledgment of any significant limitation or condition, if relevant

### Closing (final paragraph)
Must include:
- A forward-looking statement that makes the shortlist decision feel rational
- Clear indication of what happens next if the client proceeds
- Should NOT dissolve into generic "partnership" language or marketing slogans

### Length
- Target: 1-2 pages
- Absolute maximum: 3 pages
- If longer than 2 pages, every additional paragraph must earn its place with new information

## Quality Checklist

### Client Alignment
- [ ] Does the opening reference the client's specific challenge or programme?
- [ ] Does the summary use the client's own terminology (from their RFP/RFI)?
- [ ] Is the value proposition framed in terms of client outcomes, not product features?
- [ ] Are the client's stated evaluation criteria reflected in the emphasis?

### Proof Signals
- [ ] Is there at least one specific, verifiable claim (not just adjectives)?
- [ ] Is a relevant reference deployment or case study mentioned?
- [ ] Are numbers or specifics provided where they strengthen the case?
- [ ] Is the "Complexity of Doing It Right" positioning visible?

### Honesty
- [ ] If there are significant gaps, are they acknowledged at summary level?
- [ ] Is roadmap vs. current capability clear where it matters?
- [ ] Does the tone convey confidence without overclaiming?

### Readability
- [ ] Can a non-technical executive understand the summary on first read?
- [ ] Are all acronyms either explained or so standard they need no explanation?
- [ ] Is the language precise without being dense?
- [ ] Would a business sponsor remember the key message after one read?

### Memorability
- [ ] After reading the summary, can the evaluator state in one sentence what makes this proposal different?
- [ ] Are the strongest differentiators visible in scan-readable positions?
- [ ] Would the summary survive being the only thing read before a committee discussion?

## Common Executive Summary Failures

### Failure 1: The Company Biography
Opens with founding date, office count, employee mentions, and generic industry positioning.
Client challenge appears on page 2 or later.
Impact: Wastes the highest-attention real estate in the document.

### Failure 2: The Feature Catalog
Lists 10-15 platform capabilities without connecting any to the client's specific needs.
Impact: Evaluator cannot tell why these features matter for their programme.

### Failure 3: The Brochure Summary
Reads like marketing copy: "comprehensive," "innovative," "transformative," "end-to-end."
No specific proof, no mechanism, no qualification.
Impact: Zero trust built. Evaluator assumes every vendor's summary sounds like this.

### Failure 4: The Technical Dump
Jumps into architecture details, protocol names, and implementation specifics before establishing the business case.
Impact: Business sponsor disengages. Technical readers get detail but no strategic framing.

### Failure 5: The Generic Response
Could apply to any client if names were swapped. No reference to the specific RFP requirements, regulatory environment, or client constraints.
Impact: Fails the tailoring test. Evaluator concludes the vendor did not invest effort.

## Scoring Calibration for Executive Summaries

### 5/5 Executive Summary
- Opens on the client's specific challenge within the first sentence
- Value proposition is concrete, specific, and connected to evaluation criteria
- Contains at least one memorable, verifiable proof point
- Zero unexplained jargon
- Under 2 pages
- Ends with a clear forward-looking hook
- A business sponsor could repeat the core case from memory

### 4/5 Executive Summary
- Opens on the client's context (though maybe not perfectly)
- Value proposition is clear and mostly specific
- Contains proof signals, though they could be sharper
- Minor jargon or length issues
- Generally strong but not completely memorable

### 3/5 Executive Summary
- Addresses the client's need at some level
- Value proposition exists but is somewhat generic
- Proof signals are vague or insufficient
- Length is reasonable but content could be tighter
- Gets the job done without distinguishing the vendor

### 2/5 Executive Summary
- Opens with vendor biography or generic industry commentary
- Feature-heavy with weak client connection
- No verifiable proof points
- Contains unexplained acronyms or assumes deep domain knowledge
- Evaluator struggles to extract the case

### 1/5 Executive Summary
- No executive summary, or a dense wall of technical jargon
- No visible value proposition
- No client connection
- Evaluator would stop reading after the first paragraph
