# Tone Assessment Criteria

This file defines what makes good proposal tone versus bad, with specific indicators the reviewer can check.

---

## What Good Proposal Tone Sounds Like

Good proposal tone is:
- **Calm and confident** -- states capabilities without desperation or hype
- **Specific and grounded** -- backs claims with evidence rather than adjectives
- **Client-centered** -- frames everything in terms of what the client needs, not what the vendor wants to sell
- **Honest about boundaries** -- acknowledges limitations directly, without defensive hedging or burying
- **Professional without being sterile** -- reads like a competent human wrote it, not a committee or a template engine
- **Controlled** -- consistent voice, consistent certainty level, consistent terminology across sections
- **Respectful of the reader's time** -- does not pad, repeat, or over-explain obvious points

### The Trust Test
A proposal with good tone makes the evaluator think:
"These people know what they are doing and they are being straight with us."

---

## What Bad Proposal Tone Sounds Like

Bad proposal tone is:
- **Desperate or salesy** -- over-enthusiastic language, exclamation marks, "exciting opportunity" framing
- **Evasive** -- dances around limitations instead of stating them
- **Arrogant** -- claims superiority without evidence, dismisses alternatives
- **Generic** -- could apply to any vendor, any client, any situation
- **Inconsistent** -- shifts between marketing copy, technical notes, and legal hedging within the same section
- **Patronizing** -- explains things the client obviously already knows
- **Insecure** -- over-qualifies everything, hedges every statement, sounds uncertain about its own product

### The Doubt Test
A proposal with bad tone makes the evaluator think:
"Are they trying to impress me, or are they trying to answer my question?"

---

## Specific Tone Indicators

### Positive Indicators (look for these)
1. Opening sentences that reference the client's challenge, not the vendor's history
2. Capability claims followed immediately by mechanism or evidence
3. Limitations stated plainly: "The platform does not currently support X. The interim approach is Y."
4. Qualification language that clarifies rather than retreats: "In EVM-compatible environments" rather than "potentially in some cases"
5. Active voice that names who does what
6. Consistent terminology for the same concepts across sections
7. Forward-looking statements clearly labeled as roadmap: "Targeted for Q3 2026" rather than blurred tense
8. Evidence presented calmly, without "impressive" or "remarkable" framing

### Negative Indicators (flag these)
1. **Hype language**: "revolutionary," "game-changing," "paradigm shift," "next-generation," "cutting-edge"
2. **Empty superlatives**: "industry-leading," "best-in-class," "world-class," "unmatched," "unparalleled"
3. **Desperation signals**: "We would be delighted/thrilled/honored to...", excessive eagerness
4. **Vague reassurance**: "rest assured," "you can count on us," "we are fully committed"
5. **Marketing slogans in technical sections**: mixing sales copy into architecture or compliance descriptions
6. **Passive hedging accumulation**: "may potentially be able to support" instead of a clear yes/no/partial
7. **Self-congratulation**: spending paragraphs on awards, rankings, or press mentions without connecting to client value
8. **Fear-based selling**: implying the client will fail without this specific vendor

## Tone Scoring Integration

Tone affects multiple scoring dimensions:
- **Executive Readability**: Does the tone invite reading or create resistance?
- **Technical Credibility**: Does the tone suggest engineering discipline or marketing theater?
- **Honesty and Transparency**: Does the tone signal openness or evasion?
- **Client-Centricity**: Does the tone center the client or the vendor?
- **Writing Quality**: Is the tone consistent and controlled?
- **Competitive Differentiation**: Does the tone differentiate through substance or through adjectives?

### Scoring Guidance
- Consistent, confident, evidence-backed tone across sections: no deduction
- Occasional tone lapses in non-critical sections: Minor defect
- Marketing-style tone in technical or compliance sections: Moderate defect
- Pervasive hype language or evasive tone throughout: Major defect
- Tone that creates accidental overcommitment or misleading impressions: Major to Critical defect

## The "Complexity of Doing It Right" Tone

SettleMint's core positioning is about the complexity of doing institutional-grade digital assets right.
The proposal tone should reflect this:
- Acknowledge that the domain is genuinely complex
- Show that DALP exists because the hard parts matter
- Avoid oversimplifying -- regulated environments need governance, identity, compliance, and auditability
- Sound like experts who have done this before, not marketers selling a dream

The right tone is: "This is hard. We know how to do it. Here is the evidence."
The wrong tone is: "This is easy with our platform. Everything is seamless and effortless."
