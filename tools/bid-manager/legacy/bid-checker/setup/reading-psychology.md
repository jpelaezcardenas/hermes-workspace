# Reading Psychology & Evaluator Behavior

This file explains how evaluators actually read proposal documents in the real world.
Not how writers imagine they read them.
Not how a perfect academic reader would read them.
How tired, time-constrained, comparison-driven procurement people actually process a bid when three more documents are waiting.

Use this file to sharpen scoring for:
- **Executive Readability**
- **Document Flow & Structure**
- second-order effects on **Writing Quality**, **Visual Communication**, and **Client-Centricity**

Cross-reference:
- `setup/structure-patterns.md`
- `setup/writing-standards.md`
- `setup/evaluator-personas.md`
- `setup/scoring-rubric.md`

## 1. Core reality: evaluators do not read linearly

Proposal teams often imagine an evaluator sitting down, reading page 1 to page 80 in order, studying every sentence with equal care, and rewarding thoroughness.

That is fantasy.

Real evaluators:
- skim first
- anchor on early impressions
- jump to sections relevant to their role
- return later to verify claims
- compare what they just saw against another vendor's answer
- lose attention when structure forces unnecessary effort
- penalise documents that make them work too hard

A proposal is therefore read in **layers**:
1. **Orientation scan** — what is this document, who is it for, where do I find what matters?
2. **Trust scan** — does this feel credible, tailored, and professionally assembled?
3. **Targeted deep reads** — selected sections are read more closely depending on evaluator priorities.
4. **Comparison read** — the proposal is mentally or literally compared with competing bids.
5. **Recall test** — later in committee discussion, only some claims and impressions survive.

That matters because a document can be technically complete and still lose if:
- the key message is hard to spot in the first scan
- the strongest proof is buried too late
- section ordering fights evaluator workflow
- headings fail as retrieval cues
- important distinctions are not memorable enough to survive comparison

### Review implication
When judging proposal quality, ask:
- Can a useful first impression be formed in under one minute?
- Can role-specific readers find their section fast?
- Will key strengths survive later discussion from memory?
- Does the document reduce or increase evaluator effort?

If the answer is no, score down **Executive Readability** and **Document Flow & Structure** even if the content exists somewhere.

## 2. The procurement reading environment

Evaluators do not read in ideal laboratory conditions.
They read in a noisy decision environment:
- multiple vendor responses
- conflicting internal views
- time pressure
- deadline compression
- fatigue
- context switching between legal, technical, and commercial material
- varying subject-matter depth across committee members

This environment creates four predictable behaviors.

### Behavior A: selective attention
Readers notice what is:
- early
- visually prominent
- easy to decode
- emotionally or professionally relevant
- linked to a current concern

They miss what is:
- buried mid-paragraph
- framed vaguely
- presented in low-contrast prose blocks
- separated from its evidence
- hidden after long generic setup

### Behavior B: cognitive triage
Evaluators subconsciously sort content into:
- **important, read now**
- **important, check later**
- **probably boilerplate**
- **skip unless challenged**

Documents that look generic get triaged downward quickly.
Documents that signal relevance and discipline get more attention.

### Behavior C: trust compounding
Early evidence of care creates interpretive generosity.
Early evidence of sloppiness creates interpretive suspicion.

One good opening section can buy patience.
One obvious overclaim can poison the rest of the read.

### Behavior D: memory compression
After reading, evaluators do not retain pages.
They retain compressed judgments such as:
- “clear but thin on proof”
- “strong architecture, weak summary”
- “generic”
- “honest about gaps”
- “felt safer than vendor B”

Your review should therefore examine not only whether information exists, but what compressed impression the document leaves.

## 3. The first-scan model

Before a proposal is deeply read, it is scanned.
The scan is usually ruthless.

A typical first scan includes:
1. cover/title page
2. executive summary or equivalent opening
3. table of contents
4. section headings
5. diagrams, tables, and callouts
6. requirement matrix, if present
7. a few random sections of high interest

This first scan answers five silent evaluator questions:
1. **Did these people understand us?**
2. **Can I navigate this quickly?**
3. **Does this look complete and disciplined?**
4. **Do I trust the tone?**
5. **Is there something distinctive here?**

If a proposal fails these questions on scan, the rest of the read happens under a cloud.

### Review implication
When doing the skim pass, capture:
- what is obvious immediately
- what is invisible but should be obvious
- what feels generic at first glance
- whether the table of contents alone suggests a coherent argument
- whether visuals help or merely decorate

Do not dismiss first impressions as superficial.
In procurement, first impressions influence what gets read generously versus skeptically.

## 4. F-pattern scanning in proposal evaluation

The F-pattern comes from web usability research, but the underlying behavior matters in documents too.
Readers often:
- read the top horizontal line of a page or section with more attention
- read the next horizontal line with less attention
- then scan vertically down the left side looking for anchors

In proposals, this means evaluators pay disproportionate attention to:
- headings
- subheadings
- opening sentences
- left-aligned bullets
- table row labels
- diagram titles
- bolded lead-ins

They pay less attention to:
- buried middle sentences
- deep subordinate clauses
- weak paragraph endings
- unlabelled diagrams
- long prose blocks with no entry points

### Practical consequences
A section can fail even if its content is good, because the scan path never exposes the good part.

#### Strong section design under F-pattern behavior
- heading says exactly what the section will prove
- first sentence states the answer, not the preamble
- paragraph opens with the client-relevant point
- evidence follows immediately after the claim
- bullets use informative lead-ins, not vague nouns

#### Weak section design under F-pattern behavior
- heading is generic: “Approach,” “Capabilities,” “Solution Overview”
- paragraph opens with company throat-clearing
- claim is postponed to sentence five
- evidence sits on the next page
- bullets begin with abstract labels like “Flexibility,” “Innovation,” “Scalability” without context

### Review prompts
Ask:
- if I only read headings, first sentences, and diagram titles, what argument do I extract?
- are the key messages placed in scan-visible zones?
- does the left edge of the document carry meaning or wallpaper?

### Scoring impact
If scan-visible elements are vague, score down **Executive Readability** and **Document Flow & Structure**.
Even if the full text is decent, the document is fighting real reading behavior.

## 5. Serial position effect: primacy and recency

Humans remember beginnings and endings better than middles.
This is the serial position effect.
In proposal work, it matters at three levels:
- document level
- section level
- list level

### Document-level primacy
What appears early shapes trust, expectations, and reading energy.
A proposal that opens with generic company history wastes the highest-attention real estate in the entire document.

### Document-level recency
What appears near the end influences final recall before scoring discussions.
A strong close can reinforce confidence.
A weak close can leave the last taste of generic self-praise.

### Section-level primacy and recency
Within a section, readers best remember:
- the opening sentence or two
- the final takeaway or transition

The middle must still work, but openings and closings carry disproportionate weight.

### List-level primacy and recency
In bullet lists and numbered lists, first and last items often get remembered more than the middle items.
That means critical proof should not always be item 4 of 7.

### Review implication
Ask:
- what opens the document, and does it deserve that position?
- what closes the executive summary, and does it create momentum?
- do sections end with a meaningful takeaway or just stop?
- are the strongest points buried in the middle of long lists?

### Scoring impact
A proposal with strong middle content but weak opening and weak closing should not score as high as a proposal with deliberate primacy/recency management.
That should influence:
- **Executive Readability**
- **Document Flow & Structure**
- sometimes **Competitive Differentiation**

## 6. Attention decay curves in long proposals

Attention is not stable across a long read.
It decays.

Readers begin with the highest available attention.
Then attention drops because of:
- time spent
- repetition
- low information density
- visual monotony
- effortful navigation
- stress from parallel work

In practical terms, a 60-page proposal must assume that page 42 is being read by a more tired brain than page 4.

### What accelerates attention decay
- long generic introductions
- repeated value propositions with no new substance
- prose blocks without structural relief
- unexplained jargon clusters
- inconsistent formatting that disrupts reading rhythm
- heading systems that force re-orientation
- repeated claims unsupported by fresh evidence

### What slows attention decay
- clear hierarchy
- meaningful subheadings
- information chunking
- short summaries before dense material
- diagrams that truly compress complexity
- transitions that preserve orientation
- periodic evidence, not just assertions
- section openings that explain why the next section matters

### The fatigue tax
A document may be acceptable at page 5 and punishing at page 35.
That matters because evaluators do not grade average effort; they grade lived effort.

### Review implication
During review, note where reading energy drops.
Typical warning signs:
- you start skimming because the document trained you to expect low signal
- multiple pages pass without a memorable proof point
- later sections feel harder than they should because structure weakens
- the same idea is repeated in slightly different language

That is not just “a bit wordy.”
It is a performance defect.

## 7. Cognitive load theory applied to proposals

Cognitive load theory distinguishes between:
- **intrinsic load** — the inherent complexity of the topic
- **extraneous load** — the avoidable difficulty created by poor presentation
- **germane load** — the effort that helps the reader build useful understanding

Proposal writers cannot remove all intrinsic load from complex topics like settlement architecture, regulatory workflows, or operating models.
They can reduce extraneous load.
That is the job.

### Intrinsic load in proposals
Some proposal content is naturally complex:
- multi-party transaction flows
- regulatory controls
- integration architecture
- phased implementation plans
- exception handling and governance structures

Complexity itself is not a flaw.
Unmanaged complexity is.

### Extraneous load: the real enemy
Extraneous load comes from bad writing and bad structure, for example:
- abstract headings
- overloaded sentences
- undefined acronyms
- evidence separated from claims
- diagrams that add detail without explanation
- inconsistent terminology for the same concept
- tables that require constant back-and-forth decoding

### Germane load: useful effort
A good proposal makes the reader work only where that work creates understanding.
Examples:
- a well-labeled architecture diagram that rewards close attention
- a requirement matrix that maps claims to evidence
- a phased implementation timeline with dependency notes

### Review heuristic
Ask of every difficult section:
- Is this hard because the subject is hard?
- Or is it hard because the writing is careless?

If the second, penalise.

### Scoring impact
High extraneous load should reduce:
- **Executive Readability**
- **Document Flow & Structure**
- **Writing Quality**

And it may indirectly hurt:
- **Technical Credibility**
- **Client-Centricity**

because readers stop engaging deeply enough to see the merits.

## 8. Information processing limits and chunking

Humans can only keep a limited amount of information active at once.
Miller's “7±2” is often overstated as a precise law, but the practical point stands: working memory is constrained.
Many modern studies suggest the effective limit can be even lower depending on complexity.

In proposal work, this means readers cannot efficiently hold:
- seven competing claims
- three acronyms
- two unstated assumptions
- and a cross-reference to another section

all inside one paragraph.

### What chunking means here
Chunking is not just shortening text.
It is organising information into coherent units the brain can process and retrieve.

Good chunks:
- one question, one answer
- one claim, then its proof
- one process stage per subsection
- one decision point per table row
- one reader need per heading

Bad chunks:
- multiple unrelated points under one heading
- paragraphs that mix requirement answer, product description, and implementation detail
- bullets that bundle status, proof, caveat, and marketing line together

### Signs of good chunking
- sections are named around evaluator questions
- lists are grouped by logic, not by whatever came to mind
- paragraphs rarely exceed one core idea plus supporting evidence
- diagrams isolate stages or layers clearly
- repeated structural templates help the reader predict what comes next

### Signs of bad chunking
- paragraphs exceed the reader's ability to summarise them in one sentence
- subheadings appear too late or not at all
- a single section covers purpose, process, roles, architecture, and governance without separation
- the same concept is split unpredictably across multiple pages

### Review implication
Ask:
- what is the unit of thought here?
- can the reader name it quickly?
- are there too many moving parts per paragraph or per subsection?

A chunking failure is a structure failure, not just a prose preference.

## 9. The 20-second test

The 20-second test asks:
**If an evaluator gave this document 20 seconds of real attention, what would they notice?**

In those first 20 seconds, evaluators typically register:
- title and client fit
- whether the opening is client-centered or vendor-centered
- visual professionalism
- clarity of section labels
- whether the document seems easy to navigate
- whether any distinctive proof or differentiator is immediately visible

### What good proposals show in 20 seconds
- the client's challenge is visible early
- the response looks tailored
- headings are concrete
- the summary is readable
- there is evidence or specificity near the surface
- the document feels controlled, not assembled

### What bad proposals show in 20 seconds
- company chest-thumping
- template language
- generic headings
- dense walls of text
- too many bullets with no narrative frame
- “brochure energy” instead of bid discipline

### Review exercise
Run the 20-second test deliberately:
1. look only at the first page or two
2. glance at the table of contents
3. scan some headings and a major visual
4. stop
5. write what you think the vendor's case is

If you cannot articulate the case, the proposal is failing early.

### Example

**Weak first 20 seconds**
- cover page with generic subtitle
- opening paragraph about the vendor's founding date
- subheading “About SettleMint”
- no visible reference to client need

**Strong first 20 seconds**
- title references the client's programme or requirement
- opening sentence frames the client's challenge
- visible compliance matrix or solution architecture map
- a concise executive summary with clear signposts

### Scoring impact
The 20-second test belongs mainly inside **Executive Readability**, but repeated failures also affect **Document Flow & Structure** and **Client-Centricity**.

## 10. Fatigue and evaluator drift across the day

Procurement evaluators often read several proposals in a day.
By proposal four or five, tolerance changes.

Fatigue causes:
- lower patience for ambiguity
- harsher reactions to clutter
- reduced willingness to infer meaning charitably
- stronger dependence on headings and summaries
- higher reliance on first impressions and shortcuts

In other words, a document that is merely “fine if you spend time with it” becomes weaker in a fatigued reading environment.

### Fatigue-sensitive defects
These defects get punished more severely later in the day:
- vague headings
- unexplained acronyms
- redundant paragraphs
- overlong compliance answers
- generic visuals
- anything that forces backtracking

### Fatigue-resistant design
These features hold up better under fatigue:
- immediate answers
- strong chunking
- visible evidence near the claim
- selective use of tables and diagrams
- explicit labels for gaps or partial coverage
- short orienting summaries before dense sections

### Review implication
When evaluating a proposal, ask not only “can this be understood?” but “can this be understood by a tired evaluator on their fifth document?”

That is the harsher but more realistic standard.

## 11. Recognition beats recall

Readers prefer recognition over recall.
Recognition means seeing cues that help them identify meaning quickly.
Recall means having to remember and reconstruct meaning from memory.

Good proposals support recognition by using:
- descriptive headings
- consistent naming
- numbered requirements
- repeated structural patterns
- specific cross-references
- tables with explicit labels

Bad proposals force recall by using:
- vague references like “as discussed above”
- inconsistent names for the same component or concept
- sudden terminology shifts
- unnumbered sections
- headings that do not reveal content

### Example

**Recognition-friendly**
“See Section 5.3, ‘Operational controls for participant onboarding,’ for the approval workflow and segregation-of-duties model.”

**Recall-heavy**
“As mentioned earlier, governance is addressed elsewhere.”

### Review implication
If readers must remember where something was instead of recognising it instantly, structure is underperforming.

## 12. The evaluator's trust economy

Attention and trust are linked.
A reader who trusts the document gives it more attention.
A reader who doubts it starts looking for errors.

Trust increases when the document appears:
- organised
- candid
- tailored
- specific
- visually controlled
- consistent across sections

Trust falls when the document appears:
- overclaimed
- vague
- patchworked
- bloated
- evasive about limitations
- lazy in formatting or navigation

### Early trust cues
These cues matter before technical depth is even tested:
- correct use of client terminology
- alignment between table of contents and actual structure
- precise headings
- no obvious copy-paste artefacts
- sensible sequence of sections
- proportionate length of summary versus detail

### Early doubt cues
- brochure-like executive summary
- generic “About Us” lead
- all-green compliance language with no nuance
- sudden style shifts between sections
- “innovative / robust / comprehensive” adjective stacks

### Review implication
Trust is not a separate scoring dimension, but it influences nearly all of them.
A document that trips trust alarms will be read more skeptically across:
- **Technical Credibility**
- **Honesty & Transparency**
- **Client-Centricity**
- **Competitive Differentiation**

## 13. Why headings matter more than writers think

Many evaluators use headings as retrieval devices and decision cues.
A heading is not decoration.
It is a promise, a locator, and a compressed argument.

A good heading helps the evaluator answer:
- what question is being answered here?
- why should I read this section?
- if I return later, can I find this point again?

A bad heading creates friction because the evaluator must read body text to understand what kind of section it is.

### Strong headings do three things
1. identify the topic
2. signal the angle or conclusion
3. support later retrieval during discussion

### Weak headings fail one or more of those three jobs

**Strong:**
- “How DALP supports deterministic settlement finality”
- “Operational controls for issuer onboarding and approval”
- “Gaps, workarounds, and roadmap commitments for reporting”

**Weak:**
- “Solution”
- “Operations”
- “Future capabilities”

### Review implication
Headings belong inside reading psychology because many evaluators are reading them before they read the prose.
If the heading system is weak, the proposal is already leaking value.

## 14. Navigation friction and micro-disorientation

Micro-disorientation happens when the reader repeatedly has to ask:
- where am I?
- why is this here?
- what happened to the earlier thread?
- is this new, repeated, or contradictory?

Each moment is small.
But the cumulative effect is large.

### Causes of micro-disorientation
- inconsistent section numbering
- abrupt transitions
- weak cross-references
- duplicated content in different sections
- subheadings that do not match the content beneath them
- visuals inserted without introduction or interpretation

### Why it matters
Readers often do not consciously say “I am experiencing navigational friction.”
They instead conclude:
- “the document feels messy”
- “this is harder than it should be”
- “the vendor may not have thought this through”

That is a structural and trust problem disguised as a reading problem.

## 15. Comparison reading: the proposal is not read in isolation

Evaluators compare proposals across the same criteria.
Even when they do not literally place them side by side, their memory does.

That means a proposal is often judged comparatively on:
- speed of understanding
- obviousness of tailoring
- honesty about gaps
- ease of retrieving evidence
- memorability of differentiators

A proposal that is “good enough alone” may lose to a proposal that is easier to absorb and easier to defend in committee.

### Review implication
Ask not just “is this clear?” but “is this clearer than the competing norm?”

Questions:
- if another vendor frames the same requirement in half the time, does this version still compete?
- if another vendor shows the same architecture with a clean diagram, does this prose wall survive?
- if another vendor explicitly labels current capability vs roadmap, does this ambiguity now look worse?

This comparative lens should tighten standards for mediocre-but-serviceable drafting.

## 16. How psychology should influence Executive Readability scoring

Executive Readability is not about whether an executive could understand the text with enough effort.
It is about whether the proposal respects actual executive reading behavior.

### Signals of strong executive readability
- opening frames the client's problem immediately
- the value proposition is visible in the first scan
- summary is under control, not bloated
- jargon is either absent or lightly explained
- section sequence lets a non-technical sponsor grasp the case quickly
- summary ends with a clean strategic takeaway

### Signals of weak executive readability
- opening focuses on vendor biography
- important points are buried after long setup
- summary is too dense for skim reading
- multiple undefined acronyms appear early
- key distinctions require deep reading to uncover
- long lists substitute for judgment and synthesis

### Psychology-based downgrade rules
Downgrade Executive Readability when:
- the first 20 seconds fail to convey the basic case
- headings and opening sentences do not expose the argument
- the summary creates unnecessary cognitive load
- fatigue would materially worsen comprehension
- the strongest proof is buried too late to shape first impression

### Score calibration examples

**Score 5 pattern**
A business sponsor can scan the first two pages, understand the problem, solution, and why this vendor is credible, and later recall the core case in committee.

**Score 3 pattern**
The summary is competent but not scan-optimized. The reader can understand it, but only by reading more carefully than a real executive usually will.

**Score 2 pattern**
The summary contains the necessary content somewhere, but the reading path is heavy, generic, or jargon-saturated. Real executive readers will disengage.

## 17. How psychology should influence Document Flow & Structure scoring

Document Flow & Structure is not just about having sections in an order that seems vaguely sensible.
It is about whether the order, hierarchy, chunking, transitions, and retrieval cues support real evaluator cognition.

### Signals of strong flow and structure
- the table of contents reveals a legible argument
- sections follow evaluator logic: problem → approach → proof → implementation → risk/operations
- headings are descriptive enough for scan reading
- section openings orient the reader
- section endings either summarise or hand off cleanly
- cross-references reduce re-reading effort
- repeated section types use parallel structure

### Signals of weak flow and structure
- sequence reflects internal writing order rather than evaluator needs
- sections feel like separate memos stitched together
- headings are vague or inconsistent
- large sections contain multiple unrelated ideas
- transitions are missing, causing topic jumps
- later sections repeat earlier points without advancing the case

### Psychology-based downgrade rules
Downgrade Document Flow & Structure when:
- the document forces recall instead of supporting recognition
- section architecture increases extraneous cognitive load
- chunking is poor enough to exceed working-memory limits
- the middle of the document collapses into repetitive or unstructured detail
- navigation friction would worsen under fatigue

### Score calibration examples

**Score 5 pattern**
A tired evaluator can move through the document with low friction, find what they need fast, and understand how each section advances the vendor's case.

**Score 3 pattern**
The document is navigable, but the flow is merely competent. Sections make sense in sequence, yet the document does not consistently reduce reader effort.

**Score 2 pattern**
The document contains the right broad topics but feels assembled rather than architected. Readers must work too hard to follow or retrieve the argument.

## 18. Reviewer operating checklist for reading psychology

Use this checklist during the skim pass and again during structural scoring.

### First-scan checklist
- [ ] Can I identify the client's challenge inside the first 20 seconds?
- [ ] Can I tell what the vendor is proposing without reading deeply?
- [ ] Do the table of contents and headings reveal a coherent case?
- [ ] Is there an immediate trust cue: evidence, specificity, honest framing, or tailored context?
- [ ] Is there an immediate doubt cue: generic copy, vague headings, overclaiming, brochure language?

### Cognitive load checklist
- [ ] Are difficult sections difficult for legitimate subject-matter reasons, or because presentation is poor?
- [ ] Are claims and their evidence kept close together?
- [ ] Are concepts chunked into digestible units?
- [ ] Are acronyms and specialist terms explained at first use?
- [ ] Is the hierarchy doing real work, or just decorating the page?

### Fatigue checklist
- [ ] Would this still read cleanly as the evaluator's fifth proposal of the day?
- [ ] Are headings and summaries strong enough to survive skim reading under fatigue?
- [ ] Do later sections maintain discipline, or do they sprawl?
- [ ] Does the document become harder to read as it progresses?

### Recall checklist
- [ ] What three things would an evaluator likely remember later?
- [ ] Are those the right three things?
- [ ] Are the strongest differentiators placed where they will be retained?
- [ ] Are section names specific enough to support later retrieval?

## 19. Common psychological failure patterns

### Pattern 1: buried value
The proposal contains a strong point, but it appears too late or too deep in the paragraph to influence early judgment.

**Evaluator effect:** “I didn't see the point quickly enough.”

**Fix:** move claim and evidence earlier; rewrite heading; make proof scan-visible.

### Pattern 2: brochure opening
The proposal spends its highest-attention pages talking about itself in generic terms.

**Evaluator effect:** “They are selling at me, not answering me.”

**Fix:** open on client challenge and decision stakes.

### Pattern 3: section island syndrome
Each section works locally but not globally.

**Evaluator effect:** “This feels assembled.”

**Fix:** add transitions, parallel section structure, and cross-references.

### Pattern 4: overpacked compliance answers
One response bundles status, qualification, evidence, caveat, and roadmap with no chunking.

**Evaluator effect:** “I can't tell whether this is a yes, partial, or dodge.”

**Fix:** separate into clear fields or subcomponents.

### Pattern 5: repetitive reassurance
The document keeps repeating the same claim in slightly different words.

**Evaluator effect:** attention decay accelerates; trust drops.

**Fix:** say it once well, then advance the argument.

### Pattern 6: visual dead weight
There are visuals, but they do not reduce load.

**Evaluator effect:** “More stuff to decode.”

**Fix:** ensure each visual compresses complexity or aids retrieval.

## 20. Good vs bad examples

### Example A: opening section

**Bad**
“SettleMint is a leading provider of blockchain solutions with global offices and extensive experience in digital transformation across multiple industries.”

**Why it fails psychologically**
- wastes primacy
- generic
- no client relevance
- no proof
- easy to triage as brochure content

**Better**
“Your RFP asks for a platform that can support regulated digital bond issuance, deterministic settlement, and multi-jurisdiction operational control without forcing a premature ledger decision. This response explains how DALP addresses those three needs, where current coverage is partial, and what evidence supports the proposed delivery approach.”

**Why it works psychologically**
- immediate client alignment
- tells the reader what matters
- sets expectation for honest coverage
- lowers cognitive load by giving a map

### Example B: heading system

**Bad headings**
- Solution
- Capabilities
- Benefits
- Operations
- Security

**Problem**
The evaluator must read the body to know what each section is really doing.

**Better headings**
- How the proposed operating model meets your settlement requirements
- Current platform capabilities, qualified gaps, and roadmap commitments
- Control model for issuance, approval, and participant onboarding
- Security architecture and operational assurance controls

**Why better**
They support recognition, retrieval, and selective reading.

### Example C: dense paragraph

**Bad**
“The platform supports issuance, settlement, transfer restriction enforcement, role-based access control, lifecycle event handling, and integration through configurable APIs and adapters, making it suitable for a wide range of institutional use cases across regulated markets.”

**Problem**
Too many ideas in one unit; no priority; no client frame.

**Better**
“For your programme, three capabilities matter most: issuance control, deterministic settlement, and regulated participant access. DALP supports each today. Issuance rules can be configured at asset level, settlement finality is achieved through a deterministic validator model, and participant actions are gated through role-based approval workflows. Integration points to core systems are exposed through documented APIs and event hooks.”

**Why better**
- chunked
- prioritised
- tied to the client's context
- easier to retain

## 21. Practical review notes to include in reports

When reading psychology issues are present, use language tied to evaluator behavior, not abstract style commentary.

Prefer:
- “The section answers the question eventually, but the answer is buried too late to shape a first scan.”
- “The table of contents does not reveal a coherent argument, which increases navigation friction for role-specific readers.”
- “This paragraph combines too many distinct points for a tired evaluator to hold at once.”
- “The proposal's strongest evidence appears after several pages of generic framing, which wastes primacy.”
- “Later sections become visibly harder to process, suggesting avoidable attention decay caused by repetition and weak chunking.”

Avoid:
- “Could flow better.”
- “Needs more polish.”
- “Maybe simplify.”

Those are vague and not actionable.

---

## 22. Bottom line

Proposal quality is partly about what the document says.
It is also about what the evaluator can notice, process, trust, retrieve, and remember under real conditions.

A proposal that ignores reading psychology is forcing the client to do unnecessary work.
That work gets punished.

When in doubt, judge the document by the harsher standard:
**Would this hold up under skim reading, selective reading, fatigue, comparison, and later committee recall?**

If not, the score should reflect that.
