# European Union (MiCA framework)

Last updated: March 2026

**Country:** European Union (MiCA framework)
**Regulatory Authority:** European Commission; ESMA; EBA; national competent authorities

## Key Regulations

### Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA)
- **Date enacted / effective:** Entered into force 29 June 2023; stablecoin titles applied from 30 June 2024; broader CASP regime from 30 December 2024
- **Summary:** MiCA creates the EU-wide rulebook for issuers of asset-referenced tokens (ARTs), e-money tokens (EMTs), and other crypto-assets, plus licensing and conduct standards for crypto-asset service providers (CASPs). It is designed to replace fragmented national frameworks with a passportable regime centred on disclosure, governance, consumer protection, prudential safeguards, and market-abuse controls.
- **Scope:** Public offerings/admission to trading of crypto-assets, stablecoins, custody, exchange, execution, transfer, advice, portfolio management, and related services.

### Transfer of Funds Regulation (recast TFR)
- **Date enacted / effective:** Applied alongside MiCA from 30 December 2024 for most crypto-asset transfers
- **Summary:** The recast TFR extends travel-rule style information requirements to transfers of crypto-assets. CASPs must capture and transmit originator and beneficiary data and implement controls around unhosted wallets and sanctions/AML screening.
- **Scope:** Crypto-asset transfers, AML data transmission, sanctions screening, traceability.

### DAC8 / AML package
- **Date enacted / effective:** DAC8 adopted 2023 with member-state transposition/buildout through 2025-2026; AMLA package adopted 2024
- **Summary:** The EU is simultaneously expanding tax reporting and AML supervision. DAC8 will require reporting by crypto-asset service providers for tax transparency, while the new AML package centralizes parts of AML oversight and updates customer due-diligence expectations for the sector.
- **Scope:** Tax reporting, AML/CFT governance, beneficial ownership, high-risk customer controls.

## Licensing Requirements
A CASP authorization is required to provide regulated crypto-asset services in the EU, with passporting to other member states after notification. Issuers of ARTs and EMTs face separate authorization, governance, reserve, white paper, and prudential requirements; EMTs generally require an e-money institution or credit institution model.

## Taxonomy
MiCA distinguishes ARTs, EMTs, and other crypto-assets, while carving out crypto-assets that qualify as financial instruments, deposits, securitisations, insurance products, or pension products. Tokenized securities remain under MiFID/MiFIR and national securities law rather than MiCA.

## Key Compliance Requirements
Core requirements include white papers, client-asset segregation, prudential safeguards, ICT/security controls, complaints handling, conflicts management, insider-dealing and market-manipulation controls, travel-rule compliance, and AML/KYC under national AML rules plus the EU AML package.

## Recent Developments
2024-2025 was the operational transition from national VASP regimes to MiCA authorization. Supervisors have focused on authorization backlogs, reverse-solicitation claims, stablecoin reserve governance, and operational resilience. Procurement teams should assume increased scrutiny on outsourcing, cloud arrangements, incident reporting, and evidence of travel-rule readiness.

## Impact on RFP/Procurement
For enterprise procurement, MiCA pushes buyers toward vendors that can support passportable multi-entity deployments, policy-driven client-asset segregation, crypto-asset white-paper workflows, travel-rule integrations, and distinct treatment of MiCA assets versus tokenized securities. Buyers with EU ambitions increasingly view MiCA-readiness as table stakes rather than a premium feature.
