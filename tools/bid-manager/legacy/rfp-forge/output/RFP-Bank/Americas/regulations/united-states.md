# United States

Last updated: March 2026

**Country:** United States
**Regulatory Authority:** SEC, CFTC, FinCEN, OCC, Federal Reserve, state regulators such as NYDFS

## Key Regulations

### Bank Secrecy Act / FinCEN MSB framework
- **Date enacted / effective:** Applied to convertible virtual currency administrators/exchangers since 2013 guidance; ongoing
- **Summary:** At the federal level, many spot-crypto businesses are supervised for AML/CFT through FinCEN's money-services business framework. Registration, AML programs, suspicious activity reporting, sanctions controls, and travel-rule style obligations are foundational even where there is no unified federal crypto licence.
- **Scope:** Exchange, transfer, custody-adjacent money transmission, AML/CFT controls.

### Federal securities and commodities laws
- **Date enacted / effective:** Longstanding; applied case-by-case
- **Summary:** The SEC and CFTC continue to apply existing securities and commodities statutes to digital assets based on facts and circumstances. Whether a token is an investment contract, security, commodity, swap, or payment instrument drives registration, disclosure, market, and intermediary obligations.
- **Scope:** Token issuance, secondary trading, brokerage, derivatives, staking, and investment products.

### State regimes such as the New York BitLicense
- **Date enacted / effective:** BitLicense effective 2015; other state updates ongoing
- **Summary:** State money-transmission and virtual-currency laws remain critical in the United States. New York's BitLicense and limited-purpose trust models are especially influential for custody and exchange businesses serving institutions.
- **Scope:** State-level virtual currency business, custody, exchange, and consumer protection.

## Licensing Requirements
There is no single federal crypto licence. Firms may need FinCEN MSB registration, state money-transmitter licences, New York BitLicense or trust-company approval, broker-dealer/ATS registration, futures/commodity registrations, trust or banking approvals, or exemptions depending on the business model.

## Taxonomy
The US uses a facts-and-circumstances approach rather than a statutory crypto taxonomy. Digital assets may be treated as securities, commodities, money transmission instruments, or property for tax purposes; stablecoins can also implicate payments and banking rules.

## Key Compliance Requirements
AML/KYC, sanctions screening, suspicious activity reporting, state consumer disclosures, custody segregation, cybersecurity, recordkeeping, travel-rule processes, and legal-entity/licensing segmentation are key. Enterprise buyers also expect robust support for subpoenas, audit logs, and litigation hold.

## Recent Developments
2024-2025 brought a more policy-driven shift following court decisions, ETF developments, and growing federal legislative debate around market structure and stablecoins, but the regime remains fragmented. Procurement teams should assume continued legal uncertainty around classification and federal/state overlap.

## Impact on RFP/Procurement
US procurement is difficult because compliance architecture must be modular. Buyers want configurable state controls, institutional custody evidence, token-classification guardrails, and the ability to ring-fence activities by entity and licence. Platforms built only for a single “crypto exchange” use case often fail US enterprise review.
