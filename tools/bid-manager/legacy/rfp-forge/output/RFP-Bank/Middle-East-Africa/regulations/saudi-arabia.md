# Saudi Arabia

Last updated: March 2026

**Country:** Saudi Arabia
**Regulatory Authority:** Saudi Central Bank (SAMA) and Capital Market Authority (CMA)

## Key Regulations

### Fintech sandbox and supervisory pilot model
- **Date enacted / effective:** Ongoing
- **Summary:** Saudi Arabia has taken a cautious, sandbox-led approach rather than creating a broad open crypto-asset licensing regime. The authorities have prioritized controlled experimentation and financial stability over retail market liberalization.
- **Scope:** Pilots, fintech experimentation, controlled digital-asset adjacent services.

### Capital markets and securities rules
- **Date enacted / effective:** Ongoing
- **Summary:** Where tokenized products resemble securities or investment products, existing capital-markets rules and the CMA perimeter become relevant. This is more important for enterprise tokenization than for speculative spot-crypto activity.
- **Scope:** Security tokens, investment products, capital markets activity.

### AML/CFT and payments restrictions
- **Date enacted / effective:** Ongoing
- **Summary:** Saudi policy has remained conservative on payment use of decentralized crypto-assets, while still requiring strong AML and sanctions controls for any regulated experimentation. Payment-token style use cases therefore face more friction than tokenized-finance pilots.
- **Scope:** AML/KYC, payments restrictions, sanctions, banking controls.

## Licensing Requirements
There is not yet a broad retail-facing virtual-asset licence comparable to VARA or Bahrain. Institutional projects generally proceed through sandboxes, capital-markets structures, or tightly controlled regulated-entity channels.

## Taxonomy
Saudi Arabia does not operate a mature omnibus token taxonomy. In practice, the key distinction is between restricted/unapproved crypto use and regulated capital-markets or sandbox-approved activity.

## Key Compliance Requirements
Strong AML/KYC, sanctions, governance, bank-interface controls, and approvals governance are essential. Because the regime is cautious, evidence of controlled scope and risk containment matters more than broad product functionality.

## Recent Developments
2024-2025 has seen continued fintech experimentation and interest in tokenization, but no major opening for general crypto-market activity. Buyers should assume careful regulator engagement and limited appetite for uncontrolled retail use cases.

## Impact on RFP/Procurement
Procurement in Saudi Arabia favours conservative, permissioned platforms with strong governance and bank/capital-markets integration. Vendors pitching broad exchange-style functionality are usually solving the wrong problem.
