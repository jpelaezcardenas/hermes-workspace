# Chile

Last updated: March 2026

**Country:** Chile
**Regulatory Authority:** Comisión para el Mercado Financiero (CMF), Banco Central de Chile (BCCh), UAF, SII

## Key Regulations

### Fintech Law No. 21,521
- **Date enacted / effective:** Enacted October 12, 2022; published Official Gazette January 4, 2023; implementation phased through 2024–2026
- **Summary:** Chile's primary regulatory framework for fintech and digital assets. Law 21,521 explicitly recognises "crypto-assets" (virtual financial assets that use distributed-ledger technology), introduces a formal definition, and creates a licensed category of financial service providers covering: exchanges, custodians, financial instrument intermediaries, investment advisers, alternative trading systems, and crowdfunding platforms. The law introduces an Open Finance System mandating interoperability and API-based data sharing across financial providers. CMF is designated as the primary regulator for crypto-asset service providers (CASPs); the Central Bank regulates fiat-backed stablecoins for prudential purposes.
- **Scope:** CASP licensing, open finance, crypto-asset definition, conduct standards, custody, advisory.

### General Rule No. 502 (NCG 502) — CMF Implementation Framework
- **Date enacted / effective:** January 12, 2024
- **Summary:** NCG 502 is the principal CMF implementing regulation under the Fintech Law. It details: the CMF's Financial Service Providers Registry (RPSF); registration and authorisation procedures; governance and risk management requirements; AML/CFT obligations; capital adequacy; and conduct standards for crypto-asset service providers. Unlicensed operation of covered services after February 2025 is subject to daily penalties and compulsory account closure. Violations of AML regulations can result in fines of up to 1,000 UF (~USD 40,000) and criminal liability.
- **Scope:** CASP registration, governance, risk, AML, capital, conduct, reporting.

### General Rule No. 559 (NCG 559) — Notification for Existing Supervised Entities
- **Date enacted / effective:** February 9, 2026; transitional compliance deadline April 30, 2026
- **Summary:** The CMF issued NCG 559 to regulate the provision of Fintech Law services by entities already supervised by the CMF that are not required to register in the RPSF. This includes insurance companies, fund managers, securities intermediaries, credit rating agencies, and product exchanges/brokers. These entities must notify the CMF via the CMF Supervisa platform before commencing covered services. For those already providing services as of February 9, 2026, the notification deadline is April 30, 2026. Banks are subject to separate specific regulation to be issued by the CMF.
- **Scope:** Existing supervised entities, prior notification, transitional compliance.

### Circular No. 62 (RAN) — Updated AML Compilation
- **Date enacted / effective:** March 19, 2025
- **Summary:** Chile's Updated Compilation of Rules (RAN) circular increased KYC thresholds for crypto-asset transactions and updated AML/CFT risk management requirements for registered financial service providers, including CASPs. This update aligned Chile's crypto-sector AML standards with evolving FATF guidance.
- **Scope:** AML/KYC thresholds, risk management for CASPs.

### General Rule No. 514 (NCG 514) — Open Finance System
- **Date enacted / effective:** July 3, 2024
- **Summary:** NCG 514 implements the Open Finance System created by the Fintech Law, mandating API-based data sharing and interoperability among licensed financial providers. This system creates infrastructure for digital asset services to integrate with banks and payment systems, and could accelerate stablecoin-enabled payment rails in Chile.
- **Scope:** Open finance, API interoperability, financial data sharing.

### Central Bank Stablecoin Regulation (In Development)
- **Date enacted / effective:** Framework being developed; consultation ongoing as of 2025–2026
- **Summary:** The Central Bank of Chile is empowered to regulate the prudential aspects of fiat-backed stablecoins operating in Chile's payments system. As of early 2026, the BCCh was conducting a second CBDC pilot and consulting on stablecoin prudential requirements. USDT and USDC dominate Chile's stablecoin market, accounting for approximately 80% of volume.
- **Scope:** Stablecoin prudential regulation, payment system stability.

## Licensing Requirements
CASPs (crypto-asset service providers) must register with the CMF in the Financial Service Providers Registry (RPSF) under NCG 502. The registration includes a six-month substantive review covering solvency, governance, AML/CFT controls, and technical infrastructure. Unlicensed operation of covered services after February 2025 triggers enforcement. Existing CMF-supervised entities (e.g., brokers, fund managers) must notify the CMF under NCG 559 rather than apply for fresh RPSF registration. Banks face a separate regulatory pathway pending specific CMF rules.

## Taxonomy
Chile takes a functional approach rather than a three-tier token taxonomy (cf. MiCA). Crypto-assets used as investment vehicles fall under CMF supervision. Fiat-backed stablecoins used in payment systems fall under Central Bank prudential oversight. The Fintech Law draws this regulatory boundary between the CMF and BCCh, creating a complementary dual-regulator model. There is no equivalent to MiCA's utility token carve-out — default treatment is financial.

## Key Compliance Requirements
RPSF registration (CMF), AML/KYC aligned with Circular No. 62, capital adequacy, annual audits, governance structures, open finance API readiness, conduct standards under NCG 502, and ongoing supervisory notification obligations. Tax: crypto-asset gains are taxable under the SII framework; the annual crypto filing requirement has become more stringent with the 2024 Tax Compliance Law (No. 21,713).

## Recent Developments

**Chile's regulatory implementation accelerated materially in 2025–2026:**

- **February 2025 RPSF registration deadline:** Unlicensed CASPs faced daily penalties and account closure from February 2025. This marks the point at which Chile's Fintech Law regime became fully enforceable against the market.
- **Circular No. 62 (March 2025):** Updated AML/KYC thresholds, raising the threshold for enhanced due diligence and aligning Chile's crypto sector AML framework with current FATF standards.
- **CMF 2025–2026 Regulatory Plan (March 28, 2025):** The CMF published a two-year regulatory roadmap covering prudential requirements, market conduct, the Financial Resilience Act implementation, technical annexes for the Open Finance System, and further Fintech Law implementing rules. This plan signals continued rapid regulatory development through 2026.
- **NCG 559 — Existing supervised entities (February 9, 2026):** Extended Fintech Law obligations to the full range of CMF-supervised entities — insurers, fund managers, securities intermediaries — and set a transitional notification deadline of April 30, 2026.
- **Second CBDC pilot and stablecoin consultation (2025–2026):** The BCCh advanced its CBDC program and is developing prudential stablecoin regulations, with USDT/USDC dominant in Chile's current stablecoin market.
- **CMF consultation on yield-bearing products:** The CMF launched a consultation on regulatory treatment of yield-bearing crypto products, signalling intent to close regulatory gaps around DeFi-adjacent services.

## Impact on RFP/Procurement
Chilean buyers require platforms fully compliant with NCG 502 and the evolving RPSF framework. The CMF's 2025–2026 regulatory plan means procurement teams should budget for ongoing compliance configuration. Open Finance System readiness (API interoperability) is increasingly relevant as NCG 514 comes into effect. Platforms supporting stablecoin-based payment rails and meeting Central Bank prudential standards will have a structural advantage as BCCh stablecoin rules crystallise. Vendors must demonstrate modular compliance controls that can adapt to implementing-rule changes without major redevelopment.
