## 2. Tier 1 Global Banks (G-SIBs)

### Profile

**Examples:** JPMorgan, HSBC, BNP Paribas, UBS, Standard Chartered, Deutsche Bank, Citi, Goldman Sachs, Barclays, Société Générale

**Characteristics:**
- $500B+ in assets
- Global operations across 30+ countries
- Designated as Global Systemically Important Banks (G-SIBs) with enhanced regulatory oversight
- Massive existing technology estates with complex legacy integration requirements
- Dedicated innovation teams, often with existing blockchain POC experience
- Multiple internal technology governance committees

### Needs & Use Cases

**Primary use cases:**
1. **Bond tokenization**: Automating issuance, settlement, and lifecycle management of fixed-income products
2. **Tokenized deposits**: Programmable deposit products for corporate treasury clients
3. **Stablecoin operations**: Bank-issued stablecoins for institutional settlement
4. **Repo/securities lending**: Intraday settlement, automated collateral management
5. **Cross-border payments**: Correspondent banking optimization

**What they need from a platform:**
- Enterprise-grade reliability (99.99%+ uptime SLA)
- Multi-jurisdictional compliance (simultaneous compliance across 10+ regulatory regimes)
- Integration with existing infrastructure (SWIFT, core banking, risk systems, data warehouses)
- Scalability for institutional transaction volumes (thousands of TPS)
- Dedicated support with named account team
- Self-hosting or private cloud deployment options (data sovereignty)
- Comprehensive security posture (SOC 2, ISO 27001, regular pen testing)

### Procurement Process

**Typical flow:**
1. Innovation team identifies opportunity and runs internal exploration (3-6 months)
2. Business case development with finance and risk teams (2-4 months)
3. RFI to 10-15 vendors through procurement (1-2 months)
4. RFI evaluation → shortlist to 3-5 vendors (1-2 months)
5. RFP to shortlisted vendors (2-3 months)
6. Technical evaluation, demos, reference calls (1-2 months)
7. POC/pilot with 1-2 finalists (2-4 months)
8. Commercial negotiation and legal review (2-4 months)
9. Board/committee approval (1-2 months)
10. Contract execution and implementation kickoff

**Total: 12-24 months from first contact to contract**

**Key stakeholders:**
- Innovation/Digital Assets Head (champion)
- Group CTO or regional CTO (technical authority)
- Group CCO or Head of Regulatory Affairs (compliance gate)
- CISO (security gate)
- Head of Operations for relevant business line
- Group Procurement / Strategic Sourcing
- Investment committee or board (final approval for deals > €1M)

### Evaluation Criteria (Typical Weighting)

| Criterion | Weight | What They Focus On |
|---|---|---|
| Technical architecture | 30-35% | Scalability, integration, standards compliance |
| Security & compliance | 25-30% | SOC 2, pen testing, regulatory frameworks, key management |
| Vendor viability & experience | 15-20% | Financial stability, similar deployments, team expertise |
| Commercial terms | 10-15% | TCO, pricing predictability, contract flexibility |
| Implementation approach | 5-10% | Methodology, timeline, risk management |

### How to Win T1 Bank Business

1. **Lead with production references.** T1 banks want to know who else at their tier is using the platform. Named references are significantly more valuable than anonymized ones.
2. **Demonstrate integration depth.** Show detailed architecture for connecting DALP to SWIFT, core banking (Temenos, Finastra, SAP), risk systems, and data warehouses.
3. **Over-invest in security documentation.** Provide comprehensive security packages proactively, don't wait for them to ask.
4. **Multi-thread across stakeholders.** Build relationships with at least 5+ stakeholders across innovation, technology, compliance, security, and operations.
5. **Be patient.** T1 bank procurement cycles are 12-24 months. Maintain engagement throughout without being pushy.
6. **Offer a structured POC.** Define clear success criteria, timeline, and evaluation framework for the POC upfront.

### Competitive Landscape They're Evaluating

T1 banks typically evaluate:
- **SettleMint/DALP** vs. custom development on open-source (Hyperledger Besu, Corda)
- **R3/Corda** for specific trade finance and securities use cases
- **Fireblocks** for custody and key management (not direct competition but adjacent)
- **Internal blockchain teams** building on Hyperledger or Ethereum
- **Consulting firms** (Accenture, Deloitte, EY) proposing custom-build solutions

## 3. Regional & National Banks

### Profile

**Examples:** Rabobank, KBC, Belfius, National Bank of Oman, Bank Muscat, OCBC, DBS, Commercial Bank of Dubai, Zürcher Kantonalbank

**Characteristics:**
- $10B-$500B in assets
- Operations primarily in 1-5 countries
- Strong domestic market positions, often with government or quasi-government relationships
- Smaller technology teams than T1 banks but often more agile decision-making
- May be earlier in blockchain journey than T1 banks
- Less complex legacy integration (fewer systems)

### Needs & Use Cases

**Primary use cases:**
1. **Tokenized deposits**: Offering programmable deposit products to corporate and retail clients
2. **Bond distribution**: Participating in tokenized bond issuance (as distributor, not necessarily issuer)
3. **Trade finance**: Tokenized letters of credit, supply chain financing
4. **Real estate tokenization**: Fractional property investment products for clients
5. **Treasury management**: Internal efficiency through tokenized interbank transactions

**What they need from a platform:**
- Faster time-to-market than T1 banks expect (they need competitive advantage NOW)
- Managed/hosted deployment options (may not have infrastructure for self-hosting)
- Strong vendor support to compensate for smaller internal teams
- Regulatory compliance for their specific jurisdictions
- Proof that the platform works for banks their size (not just T1 giants)
- Cost-effective licensing that scales with their growth

### Procurement Process

**Typical flow (shorter than T1):**
1. Digital innovation or treasury team identifies opportunity (1-3 months)
2. Market research and vendor identification (1-2 months)
3. RFI/vendor screening to shortlist 3-5 vendors (1-2 months)
4. RFP or direct vendor evaluation (1-2 months)
5. Demo and POC (1-2 months)
6. Commercial negotiation (1-2 months)
7. Management committee approval (1 month)
8. Contract and kickoff

**Total: 6-12 months from first contact to contract**

### Evaluation Criteria

| Criterion | Weight | Key Difference from T1 |
|---|---|---|
| Time-to-market | 25-30% | They value speed more than T1 banks |
| Vendor support quality | 20-25% | Need more hand-holding; smaller internal teams |
| Technical fit | 20-25% | Focus on hosted/managed deployment options |
| Cost | 15-20% | More price-sensitive than T1 |
| References | 10-15% | Want to see banks their size, not just T1 names |

### How to Win Regional Bank Business

1. **Emphasize speed.** "12 weeks to first issuance" resonates strongly. They can't afford 18-month projects.
2. **Offer managed services.** Cloud-hosted, SettleMint-managed deployment removes infrastructure burden.
3. **Provide relevant references.** OCBC-level references are more relatable than JPMorgan-level.
4. **Be responsive.** Regional banks often have smaller teams. Slow responses lose deals to nimbler vendors.
5. **Demonstrate growth path.** Show how they start small (one asset class) and expand without re-implementation.
6. **Price competitively.** Offer entry-level pricing tiers that grow with usage.

### Competitive Landscape

Regional banks typically evaluate:
- **SettleMint/DALP** vs. consortium/industry solutions from local partners
- **Tokeny** (especially European banks) for securities tokenization
- **DigiShares** for real estate tokenization
- **Smaller regional consultancies** proposing custom builds
- **Their core banking vendor** (if offering DLT modules, e.g., Temenos, Finastra)

## 4. Asset Managers

### Profile

**Examples:** BlackRock, Vanguard, Schroders, Franklin Templeton, WisdomTree, Amundi, DWS, Hamilton Lane, KKR (digital assets divisions)

**Characteristics:**
- $10B-$10T+ in AUM
- Product-focused (new products = new revenue)
- Distribution is everything, more investors = more AUM = more fees
- Regulatory environment varies by product type and jurisdiction
- Often more agile than banks in technology adoption
- May already have tokenized fund pilots (BUIDL, BENJI)

### Needs & Use Cases

**Primary use cases:**
1. **Fund tokenization**: Tokenized fund units for broader distribution, lower minimums
2. **Real-world asset (RWA) tokenization**: Real estate, private credit, infrastructure funds
3. **Fractional investment products**: Lower minimum investments for institutional and retail
4. **Automated fund operations**: NAV calculation, subscription/redemption, dividend distribution
5. **Secondary market liquidity**: Enabling trading of traditionally illiquid fund units

**What they need from a platform:**
- Support for multiple fund structures (UCITS, AIF, ETF, closed-end, open-end)
- Integration with fund administration systems (transfer agents, custodians, NAV providers)
- Investor onboarding and KYC/KYB integration
- Distribution network connectivity (exchanges, brokers, platforms)
- Automated compliance for investor eligibility, concentration limits, redemption gates
- Fractional unit support with configurable decimal precision

### Procurement Process

**Typically faster than banks:**
1. Product/innovation team identifies tokenization opportunity (1-2 months)
2. Vendor research and shortlisting (1-2 months)
3. Demo and technical evaluation (1-2 months)
4. POC with preferred vendor (1-2 months)
5. Commercial negotiation and legal (1-2 months)
6. Contract and launch preparation (1 month)

**Total: 4-9 months from first contact to contract**

### Evaluation Criteria

| Criterion | Weight | Key Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Asset class coverage | 25-30% | Can the platform handle their specific fund type? |
| Distribution capabilities | 20-25% | How easily can they reach new investors? |
| Fund operations automation | 20-25% | NAV, subscriptions, redemptions, dividends |
| Compliance & regulatory | 15-20% | Investor eligibility, cross-jurisdiction |
| Pricing & TCO | 10-15% | Typically per-fund or per-AUM pricing |

### How to Win Asset Manager Business

1. **Lead with distribution value proposition.** "Expand your investor base by 10x through fractional tokenized fund units distributed across 5 exchanges."
2. **Show fund administration integration.** They won't rip out their transfer agent. Show DALP integrating alongside existing systems.
3. **Demonstrate automated operations.** Automated NAV, subscriptions, redemptions, and distributions are the killer feature.
4. **Provide fund-specific compliance.** UCITS eligibility rules, AIF investor restrictions, MiFID suitability, not generic compliance.
5. **Quick POC.** Asset managers want to tokenize a test fund within weeks, not months.

### Competitive Landscape

Asset managers typically evaluate:
- **SettleMint/DALP** for full-lifecycle platform
- **Securitize** (strong in RWA tokenization, US-focused)
- **Tokeny** (European securities tokenization)
- **Archax** (London-based exchange + tokenization)
- **Ownera / FinP2P** (distribution network focus)
- **Their custodian** (if offering tokenization services. BNY Mellon, State Street, Citi)

## 5. Central Banks

### Profile

**Examples:** ECB, Bank of England, Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS), Central Bank of UAE, Central Bank of Bahrain, Bank of Thailand, Banque de France

**Characteristics:**
- Sovereign monetary authority, ultimate risk aversion
- Longest procurement timelines of any segment
- Extremely thorough evaluation processes
- Often operating through regulatory sandboxes for blockchain experimentation
- Dual role: regulator AND potential user of DLT
- Procurement may follow government tender processes

### Needs & Use Cases

**Primary use cases:**
1. **CBDC (Central Bank Digital Currency)**: Wholesale or retail digital currency
2. **Wholesale settlement**: Interbank settlement using DLT
3. **Regulatory supervision**: Real-time monitoring of tokenized asset markets
4. **Securities settlement infrastructure**: DvP/DvP settlement for government securities
5. **Cross-border payment infrastructure**: Multi-CBDC platforms (mBridge, Mariana, etc.)

**What they need from a platform:**
- Absolute sovereignty over technology, data, and operations
- On-premises or sovereign cloud deployment (MUST)
- Source code access and ability to audit (MUST)
- No dependency on external vendor for continued operations
- Extreme performance and reliability requirements
- Multi-year support commitments (5-10+ years)
- Technology-neutral platform (not locked to one blockchain protocol)
- Supervisory tools for regulatory oversight

### Procurement Process

**Extremely rigorous:**
1. Internal research and policy development (6-12 months)
2. Regulatory sandbox experimentation (3-12 months)
3. Formal vendor market study / RFI (2-4 months)
4. Government procurement process / tender (3-6 months)
5. Technical evaluation and sandbox testing (3-6 months)
6. Security assessment and sovereign deployment validation (2-4 months)
7. Intergovernmental/board approval (2-6 months)
8. Contract negotiation (often government contract terms) (2-4 months)
9. Implementation with extensive testing (6-12 months)

**Total: 12-36 months from first contact to contract**

### Evaluation Criteria

| Criterion | Weight | Key Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Sovereignty & independence | 25-30% | Can they operate without the vendor? Source code access? |
| Security posture | 25-30% | Government-grade security, air-gapped deployment |
| Technical capability | 20-25% | CBDC-specific features, performance, interoperability |
| Vendor stability & track record | 10-15% | Longevity, similar central bank engagements |
| Cost | 5-10% | Budget matters less than capability and sovereignty |

### How to Win Central Bank Business

1. **Lead with sovereignty.** "DALP can be deployed entirely on-premises with full source code access. Your team can operate it independently with no ongoing vendor dependency."
2. **Demonstrate regulatory understanding.** Central bankers want a vendor who understands monetary policy implications, not just technology.
3. **Invest in relationship building.** Central bank procurement is 80% relationship and trust, 20% RFP score. Attend central banking conferences, publish relevant research, engage through regulatory sandboxes.
4. **Offer sandbox-first engagement.** "Deploy DALP in your regulatory sandbox with zero commitment. Evaluate at your pace."
5. **Be patient.** These cycles are 12-36 months. Maintain presence without being pushy.
6. **Provide reference calls with other central banks.** Nothing builds trust like a peer reference.

### Competitive Landscape

Central banks typically evaluate:
- **SettleMint/DALP** as a configurable platform
- **R3/Corda** (historically strong in central bank space)
- **Consensys** (Ethereum-based solutions, Hyperledger Besu)
- **IBM** (Hyperledger Fabric, consulting + technology)
- **BIS Innovation Hub** projects and frameworks
- **Custom development** (often the default for sovereignty reasons)

## 6. Securities Exchanges & CSDs

### Profile

**Examples:** Euronext, SIX Swiss Exchange (SDX), Deutsche Börse (Clearstream), DTCC, ASX, SGX, Tadawul, ADX, Nasdaq

**Characteristics:**
- Market infrastructure providers, they ARE the financial plumbing
- Extremely conservative about technology changes (systemic risk considerations)
- Deep technical expertise in settlement, clearing, and custody
- Regulatory pressure to modernize (shorter settlement cycles, T+1)
- Many running parallel DLT initiatives alongside traditional infrastructure
- Often consortium-driven decision-making

### Needs & Use Cases

**Primary use cases:**
1. **Digital asset trading infrastructure**: New exchanges or segments for tokenized securities
2. **Post-trade settlement**: DvP/FoP settlement with atomic finality
3. **CSD modernization**: Moving from traditional book-entry to tokenized registries
4. **Collateral management**: Real-time collateral movement and optimization
5. **Cross-border settlement**: Interoperability with other CSDs and market infrastructures

**What they need from a platform:**
- Ultra-high performance (10,000+ TPS, sub-second finality)
- Regulatory compliance with securities law across jurisdictions
- Integration with existing clearing, settlement, and custody systems
- Support for standard message formats (ISO 20022, FIX)
- Multi-asset capability across securities types
- Participant management and network governance tools

### Procurement Process

**Formal, often consortium-driven:**
1. Strategic assessment and board mandate (3-6 months)
2. Market research and vendor landscape review (2-4 months)
3. Formal RFI through procurement (1-2 months)
4. RFP to shortlisted vendors (2-3 months)
5. Technical evaluation and prototype testing (3-6 months)
6. Regulatory consultation on proposed model (2-4 months)
7. Board approval and commercial negotiation (2-4 months)
8. Phased implementation with extensive parallel running (6-18 months)

**Total: 9-18 months from first contact to contract, plus 6-18 months implementation**

### Evaluation Criteria

| Criterion | Weight | Key Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Performance & reliability | 30-35% | TPS, latency, uptime, failover |
| Integration with market infrastructure | 25-30% | CSD interoperability, clearing integration |
| Regulatory compliance | 20-25% | Securities regulation, CSDR, MiFID II |
| Participant management | 10-15% | Onboarding, governance, network management |
| Commercial model | 5-10% | Revenue sharing, licensing, per-transaction pricing |

### How to Win Exchange/CSD Business

1. **Performance benchmarks are everything.** Provide independently verified performance data. Exchanges won't accept theoretical numbers.
2. **Demonstrate market infrastructure understanding.** Speak their language: DvP, FoP, CCP clearing, participant management, corporate actions.
3. **Show integration with existing infrastructure.** DTCC integration, CSD interoperability, ISO 20022 messaging.
4. **Address the "parallel running" question.** Show how tokenized and traditional infrastructure coexist during migration.
5. **Offer network governance tools.** Exchanges need to manage participants, enforce rules, and maintain market integrity.

## 7. Government Entities

### Profile

**Examples:** Dubai Land Department, Singapore Government (GovTech), Swiss Federal Government, Abu Dhabi Digital Authority, European Commission DG projects

**Characteristics:**
- Government procurement rules (tender processes, public competition requirements)
- Extremely bureaucratic but increasingly innovative in specific areas
- Often driven by national digital transformation agendas
- Budget cycles aligned to government fiscal years
- Local content/employment requirements may apply
- Political dynamics influence procurement decisions

### Needs & Use Cases

**Primary use cases:**
1. **Land/property registries**: Tokenized land titles, transparent property records
2. **Digital identity**: Self-sovereign identity, credential management
3. **Public asset management**: Government asset tokenization for efficiency
4. **Government bonds**: Sovereign bond issuance on DLT
5. **Trade facilitation**: Cross-border trade documentation and customs
6. **Carbon credits / ESG tracking**: Environmental asset tokenization

**What they need from a platform:**
- Compliance with government procurement regulations
- Data sovereignty (on-premises or government cloud deployment)
- Multi-stakeholder governance (multiple government agencies on one platform)
- Transparency and audit capabilities for public accountability
- Long-term vendor commitment and local presence
- Citizen/public-facing interfaces where applicable
- Open source preference common (but not universal)

### Procurement Process

**Government tender process (often rigid):**
1. Government identifies digital transformation opportunity (6-12 months)
2. Budget allocation through government fiscal planning (3-12 months)
3. Formal tender publication (RFP/RFI) through government procurement portal (1-2 months)
4. Vendor submission and evaluation (2-4 months)
5. Presentation/demo phase (1-2 months)
6. Negotiation and legal review (2-4 months)
7. Government committee/ministry approval (1-3 months)
8. Contract award and kickoff

**Total: 12-24 months (often dictated by government fiscal cycles)**

### Evaluation Criteria

| Criterion | Weight | Key Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Solution fit and capability | 30-35% | Specific use case coverage |
| Past performance | 20-25% | Government/public sector references |
| Vendor qualifications | 15-20% | Certifications, local presence, team |
| Cost | 15-20% | Often "lowest compliant price" wins |
| Local content / social value | 5-15% | Local hiring, knowledge transfer, capacity building |

### How to Win Government Business

1. **Understand the procurement rules.** Government tenders have strict process requirements. A minor non-compliance can disqualify you.
2. **Local presence matters.** Government entities strongly prefer (or require) vendors with local offices, local staff, or local partnerships.
3. **Past government references are critical.** Demonstrate experience working with government entities, even if in different countries.
4. **Price competitively.** Government procurement often heavily weights price. Build a compelling TCO story.
5. **Knowledge transfer is valued.** Offer training, capacity building, and technology transfer as part of your proposal.
6. **Be prepared for long cycles.** Government procurement doesn't move fast. Maintain engagement across fiscal years.

## 8. Insurance Companies

### Profile

**Examples:** AXA, Allianz, Munich Re, Swiss Re, Lloyd's of London, Prudential, MetLife, Zurich Insurance Group

**Characteristics:**
- Complex product lines (life, P&C, reinsurance)
- Heavy regulatory oversight (Solvency II in EU, state regulations in US)
- Traditionally conservative technology adopters
- Strong actuarial and risk management culture
- Increasing pressure to digitize claims, underwriting, and distribution
- Growing interest in parametric insurance and climate risk products

### Needs & Use Cases

**Primary use cases:**
1. **Parametric insurance**: Automated claim triggering based on predefined parameters (weather, earthquake, etc.)
2. **Reinsurance**: Tokenized risk transfer, automated treaty management
3. **Claims processing**: Automated claims verification and settlement
4. **Policy tokenization**: Tradable insurance-linked securities
5. **Fraud prevention**: Shared fraud registries and verification networks

**What they need from a platform:**
- Integration with insurance core systems (policy admin, claims management)
- Oracle integration for parametric triggers (weather data, IoT sensors, etc.)
- Smart contract capability for automated claims processing
- Compliance with insurance regulations (Solvency II, policyholder protection)
- Multi-party network support (insurer, reinsurer, broker, policyholder)
- Audit trail for regulatory reporting

### Procurement Process

**Similar to banking but often faster:**
1. Innovation or operations team identifies use case (1-3 months)
2. Vendor research and shortlisting (1-2 months)
3. RFI/RFP (1-2 months)
4. Demo and POC (1-3 months)
5. Commercial negotiation (1-2 months)
6. Compliance review and board approval (1-2 months)

**Total: 6-12 months from first contact to contract**

### Evaluation Criteria

| Criterion | Weight | Key Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Use case fit | 25-30% | Can it handle parametric / claims / reinsurance? |
| Integration capability | 20-25% | Core system connectivity, oracle integration |
| Automation capability | 20-25% | Smart contract automation for claims/triggers |
| Security & compliance | 15-20% | Solvency II, data protection |
| Cost & vendor viability | 10-15% | TCO, vendor stability |

### How to Win Insurance Business

1. **Lead with specific insurance use cases.** Generic blockchain pitches don't resonate. Show parametric insurance examples or reinsurance automation.
2. **Demonstrate oracle integration.** Parametric insurance requires reliable external data feeds. Show how DALP connects to weather APIs, IoT, and other data sources.
3. **Automate claims.** The killer value proposition is automated, instant claims settlement. Show the smart contract flow from trigger → verification → payment.
4. **Address reinsurance complexity.** Multi-party smart contracts for treaty management resonate with reinsurance executives.
5. **Build insurance-specific references.** This segment is nascent in blockchain adoption, early references carry outsized weight.

## 9. Cross-Segment Patterns

### Universal Requirements Across All Segments

Despite different priorities, all segments require:

1. **Security certifications**: SOC 2 Type II at minimum, ISO 27001 increasingly expected
2. **Data residency control**: Ability to choose where data lives
3. **Audit trail**: Immutable, exportable, regulator-ready
4. **Integration APIs**: REST/GraphQL, standard message formats
5. **Support SLAs**: Defined response times, escalation procedures
6. **Exit provisions**: Data portability, migration assistance
7. **Compliance adaptability**: Platform adapts as regulations evolve

### Segment-Specific Priority Shifts

| Priority | Banks | Asset Mgrs | Central Banks | Exchanges | Government | Insurance |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Speed to market | High | Very high | Low | Medium | Low | High |
| Sovereignty | Medium | Low | Critical | Medium | Critical | Low |
| Cost sensitivity | Low-med | Medium | Low | Medium | High | Medium |
| Integration depth | Critical | High | Medium | Critical | Medium | High |
| Performance | High | Medium | High | Critical | Medium | Medium |
| Regulatory compliance | Critical | High | Critical | Critical | High | High |
