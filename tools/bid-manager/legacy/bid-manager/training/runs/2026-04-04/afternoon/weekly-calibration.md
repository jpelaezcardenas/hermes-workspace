## Lessons Review

### Mixed-audience writing is still a revision habit, not a drafting habit

The lessons file says mixed-audience writing is mandatory. Week 3 shows this principle is understood after feedback, but not yet built into the first pass. Drafts still assume evaluators will work through the detail to discover the value. In practice, procurement and business readers need the relevance stated first, then supported with technical evidence.

### Repetition still signals weak editorial control

The lessons file warns that repetition without new evidence feels like padding. Week 3 outputs handled technical boundaries honestly, but some sections still repeated DALP's flexibility without converting that idea into a new proof point. The draft should make the argument once, then prove it differently through compliance control, lifecycle flexibility, governance change, or operational speed.

### Composability should open the case, then convert into evaluator outcomes

The lessons file correctly identifies composability as the lead differentiator. Week 3 confirms that this is the right opening frame, but not a full argument by itself. After the opening, the draft needs to explain what composability means for the buyer: lower change risk, fewer redeployments, easier adaptation to regulation, and more stable operations as requirements evolve.

### Early proof remains underused

The editorial lessons say the first visible layer shapes trust. Week 3 still treated proof too often as a revision move. In practice, proof should appear in the draft plan from the start. In a proposal context, proof means one of four things: a screenshot, a comparison table, a workflow figure, or a capability-to-requirement block. Each major section should have at least one.

### IP discipline held, but the stress test exposed the risk margin

The lessons file requires an IP protection checklist before submission. Final Week 3 outputs stayed clean, but the low IP stress score is still significant. Under pressure, the drift risk is not oversharing code. It is slipping from public-safe capability language into implementation-adjacent specificity. The fix is disciplined abstraction, not vagueness.

## Calibration Decisions for Week 4

### Scoring approach

Do not tighten the full rubric yet. Week 3 does not show general score inflation. It shows selective underperformance. Keep the overall target band at 38 to 44.

Tighten expectations only in the three persistent weak categories.

| Category | Week 4 expectation |
| --- | --- |
| Client Centricity | A section cannot score 3 unless its opening sentence clearly states client value or evaluator relevance |
| Visual / Screenshots | A technical or proposal-style draft cannot score 3 unless it includes at least two concrete proof assets planned or embedded |
| Competitive Edge | A section cannot score 3 unless it links DALP architecture to a meaningful buyer advantage |

### Week 4 draft checklist

| Check | Pass condition |
| --- | --- |
| Client-first opening | Every major section begins with why the evaluator should care |
| Proof planned before prose | Each major section has a screenshot, table, workflow figure, or comparison block assigned before drafting |
| Boundary sentence present | Any non-native capability is followed immediately by a clear boundary statement |
| Differentiation stated once, then proved | DALP's advantage appears early and is supported with fresh evidence rather than repeated wording |
| IP scan complete | Final text passes the allow and deny checks in `setup/ip-protection.md` |

### Process changes

**Client-value first sentence.** Before drafting each section, write the single sentence that explains why the evaluator should care right now. Keep that sentence in the draft.

**Proof plan before prose.** For any afternoon exercise, assign the proof format before writing. Acceptable proof formats are screenshot, table, workflow figure, or comparison block.

**Boundary sentence for non-native requirements.** When a requirement extends beyond native platform scope, place the limitation sentence immediately after the capability statement. This keeps honesty inside the argument rather than leaving it to cleanup.

**Editorial anti-repetition pass.** Before closing the document, scan for repeated differentiator phrases and replace duplicate claims with new evidence or remove them.

**IP pre-close scan.** The requested proposal-checker IP checklist file was not present in the workspace during this run. Until a dedicated checker checklist is available in a readable location, use the checklist in `setup/ip-protection.md` as the mandatory close-out gate.
