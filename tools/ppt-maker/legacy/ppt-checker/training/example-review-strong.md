## Top Issues
1. Slide 6 tries to explain the DALP control plane, policy engine, and integration model in one pass. Split the capability explanation from the technical architecture so the executive audience does not have to decode both at once.
2. Slide 9 combines customer proof with implementation timing. Pick one primary takeaway for the slide and move the secondary point to the next slide.
3. Slide 11 is slightly overbuilt for the audience. The delivery model is credible, but the six-step rollout sequence can be simplified to three phases for the live conversation.

- Good proof strategy: the deck uses architecture, controls, and deployment flexibility as evidence instead of empty superiority language.
- The close is commercially useful. It ends on a concrete next-step conversation rather than a generic thank-you slide.

## Slide-Level Notes
- Slide 1 (Cover): Strong, credible opening. “Operational infrastructure for regulated digital assets” is sharper than a generic DALP product title and sets the audience expectation well.
- Slide 2 (Executive Summary): Good use of a summary layout. The three takeaway blocks are crisp, but the third block could say “Deploy within your control boundary” instead of “Flexible deployment options” to make the benefit more concrete.
- Slide 3 (Market Problem): One of the best slides in the deck. Clear message hierarchy, good restraint, and the language reflects real operating pain: siloed lifecycle workflows, fragmented controls, and integration drag.
- Slide 4 (Why DALP): Strong fit for the template. The three-column structure cleanly ties orchestration, compliance, and interoperability to business outcomes.
- Slide 5 (Platform Overview): Clear and accurate. Good use of native shapes and labels. Minor issue: the caption under “Policy & Controls” is a little small relative to the rest of the slide.
- Slide 6 (Architecture): Credible content, but slightly too much for an executive room. The top row on platform capabilities is useful; the lower system interactions deserve their own slide.
- Slide 7 (Deployment Models): Strong audience fit. The slide does a good job showing cloud, self-hosted, and hybrid deployment options without overclaiming implementation simplicity.
- Slide 8 (Governance & Compliance): Good proof slide. The controls language is appropriately careful. Replace “institution-grade” with a more specific phrasing if possible, or back it with an actual control example.
- Slide 9 (Proof / Outcomes): Useful material, but this is where the narrative blurs. The customer outcome boxes are strong enough on their own; the rollout timeline below them should move to Slide 10.
- Slide 10 (Implementation Path): Solid structure and realistic pacing. Good that the slide frames phased rollout instead of pretending everything lands at once.
- Slide 11 (Operating Model): Valuable content, but the six swimlanes create unnecessary reading load. Condense to three ownership lanes: Business, Operations, Technology.
- Slide 12 (Call to Action / Close): Strong close. The next step is specific and commercially sane: align target workflow, control model, and deployment boundary in a working session.
