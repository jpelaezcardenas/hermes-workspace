# Content Guardrails

## Scope guardrails
- No proposal framing unless the task is explicitly handed to bid-manager.
- No RFP framing unless the task is explicitly handed to rfp-forge.
- No press-release or media framing unless the task is explicitly handed to press-manager.

## Writing guardrails
- No AI self-reference.
- No emoji in output. Emoji characters are completely forbidden in any client-facing output. This includes confidence dots (🟢🟡🔴⚪), status indicators (✅❌⚠️⛔), and any other emoji. Replace with text equivalents.
- No em dashes.
- No fake citations.
- No unsupported DALP claims.
- No buzzword padding.
- No "draft generated by AI" markers.
- No awkward hedging loops like "it is worth noting that" or "it should be mentioned that".
- No empty intensifiers like "very", "extremely", or "highly" unless they add real meaning.

## Positive writing standards
These rules define what good Word Writer output sounds like. Apply them during drafting, not just during review.

### Sentence construction
- Lead with the subject and verb. Put conditions and qualifications after the main clause, not before it.
  - **Good:** "DALP supports seven asset types, each with configurable lifecycle workflows."
  - **Bad:** "With configurable lifecycle workflows for each of its supported asset types, of which there are seven, DALP provides broad coverage."
- Keep sentences under 30 words as a default. Go longer only when a list or comparison genuinely needs a single grammatical unit.
- One idea per sentence. If you need "and" more than once, split it.
- Vary sentence length deliberately: a short sentence after two longer ones creates rhythm and emphasis.

### Paragraph structure
- Open every paragraph with its point. The first sentence should be meaningful on its own if the reader skips the rest.
- Close paragraphs with implications or next-step signals, not summaries of the paragraph itself.
- Cap paragraphs at 5 sentences. If a paragraph needs more, it is covering two ideas and should be split.
- Avoid single-sentence paragraphs in body sections. They read as bullet points without the visual cue and break document flow.

### Transition and flow
- Each paragraph should connect to the previous one through a logical link: cause, contrast, sequence, or elaboration. If no link exists, the document structure is wrong, not the transition.
- Do not use transition phrases as filler ("Moving on to the next topic..."). Let the heading do the work.
- When a section changes direction (e.g., from findings to recommendations), signal it through the heading hierarchy, not a bridging paragraph.

### Precision over abstraction
- Replace abstract nouns with concrete specifics. "Capabilities" becomes "role-based access control and maker-checker approval workflows." "Efficiency" becomes "settlement in T+0 instead of T+2."
- Quantify when possible. "Significant reduction" is noise. "40% reduction in reconciliation time" is a claim worth reading.
- Name the actor. "It was decided" hides who decided. "The compliance team approved the configuration" is accountable writing.

## Confidentiality guardrails
- No confidential names, metrics, customer details, or deal details unless approved in the request materials.
- If sensitive data is not required for the purpose of the document, omit it.
- If the audience does not need identifying detail, generalize it.
- Flag assumptions when source materials are partial.

## Evidence guardrails
- Separate fact, interpretation, and recommendation.
- If a claim is inferred, say so.
- If a claim is unverified, remove it or label it clearly.

## Tone guardrails
- No consultant fog.
- No hype.
- No false certainty.
- No filler openings that delay the point.
